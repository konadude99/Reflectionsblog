A Rigorous Clinical Investigation

A Field Guide to the Modern Doctor's Office

Sixteen documented absurdities, from the waiting room to the referral maze, with notes from the field.

▾   Scroll to begin suffering

You went in for something minor. You left confused about your medications, unsure of your blood pressure, and vaguely insulted by a paper gown. This happens to everyone. This is that story.

Incident № 01 · Front Desk

The Check-In Kiosk That Defeats Everyone

The office has replaced the receptionist with a touchscreen tablet on a wobbly stand. It takes four taps to confirm your name, asks you to sign with your finger — producing a signature that looks like a seismograph reading from a 7.2 earthquake — then freezes on the insurance screen. The receptionist, three feet away, watches you struggle for ninety seconds before saying: "Yeah, sometimes you just have to restart it."

→ The machine will not remember this. You will.

Incident № 02 · The Waiting Room

The Waiting Room Ecosystem

There are exactly four kinds of people in any waiting room at any time: (1) the person coughing without covering their mouth, whom everyone else watches with cold fury; (2) the person who brought full takeout and is eating it loudly; (3) the individual who has been there so long they appear to have achieved a kind of Zen acceptance of mortality; and (4) you, who arrived three minutes early and will wait forty-seven minutes.

→ The clock has been wrong since 2019. No one will fix it.
"Is your address still the same?" She will ask this, and then hand you a form to write it down anyway.
— Field observation, every waiting room, 2001–present

Incident № 03 · Reception Desk

"Is Your Address Still…?"

The receptionist asks if your address is still the same. You confirm it is. She asks if your phone number is still the same. You confirm it is. She asks if your emergency contact is still the same. You confirm it is. She then hands you a clipboard and asks you to fill out all of this information again, by hand, on a form that appears to be a photocopy of a photocopy of something originating in the Clinton administration.

→ This form will be scanned and filed where no human shall find it.

Incident № 04 · Examination Room

The Blood Pressure Cuff Situation

A nurse wraps the cuff around your arm, pumps it to a pressure suggesting she's checking a truck tire, and you both stare at the wall in companionable silence while your hand turns the color of a beet. She announces a number. You have no idea if it's good or alarming. She writes it down without any expression whatsoever. You spend the next ten minutes secretly googling what your number means and then googling what the google results mean.

→ It was "a little elevated." This will not be explained further.

Incident № 05 · Consultation

"On a Scale of One to Ten…"

The doctor asks you to rate your pain from one to ten. This sounds simple. It is not. Is a seven too dramatic? Is a four an insult to your own suffering? What's a ten — being crushed by a glacier? You settle on "maybe a five?" with a question mark on the end of it, as if asking permission. The doctor writes it down without comment. You immediately wonder if you should have said six.

→ You will choose a different number next time. It won't matter.

Incident № 06 · 2 A.M. Research

The Symptom You Googled at 2 A.M.

You've had a weird ache under your left shoulder blade for three weeks. Google has diagnosed you, in order, with: a pulled muscle, a herniated disc, a kidney issue, gallbladder disease, a rare autoimmune condition found primarily in Uzbekistan, and — on page four — something unspeakable. You arrive ready to write your will. The doctor presses on it once and says, "You've been sitting weird." That's it. That's the whole appointment.

→ Stop googling symptoms. You won't stop googling symptoms.

Incident № 07 · Examination Room

The Gown

You're handed a paper gown and told to "put it on with the opening in the back." There is no further instruction. The gown is approximately the size of a cocktail napkin. You arrange it around yourself as best you can, sit on the crinkly paper of the examination table, and spend eleven minutes alone in the room feeling profoundly vulnerable and slightly cold, wondering if "the back" means your back or the back of the room.

→ Either way you have chosen incorrectly. The nurse will not say so.

Incident № 08 · Medication Review

The Medication Review

"Are you taking any medications?" You list the two you actually remember. The doctor pulls up your chart, which apparently knows about four more. You didn't know you were on one of them. You're not sure when that happened. Nobody seems troubled by this. Everyone moves on. You make a mental note to look into it, which you will absolutely forget in the parking lot.

→ The chart knows things about you that you do not know about yourself.
"Is that a five, or a five-with-a-question-mark? Because I wrote five but I should have said six, and now it's in my chart forever."
— Interior monologue, every patient, forever

Incident № 09 · The Patient With a Plan

The Person Who Brings a List

There's always one person in the waiting room clutching a handwritten list of questions for their doctor. Five pages. Laminated. Cross-referenced. They have been preparing for this appointment the way other people prepare for a doctoral dissertation defense. The doctor will have twelve minutes with them. The list has thirty-four items.

→ They will get through six. They will return next week with the rest.

Incident № 10 · The Terrifying Symptom

The Frightening Symptom with the Humiliating Explanation

You've been experiencing heart palpitations. Irregular, alarming, enough to make you clutch your chest at your desk. You describe this gravely. The doctor asks what you've been drinking. You mention the new energy drink you've been having — the big one, three a day because they're on sale. The doctor closes her laptop. "Stop drinking those." You go home cured.

→ The cure cost nothing. The dignity cost everything.

Incident № 11 · Examination Room

The Examination Table Paper

Every slight shift of position produces the sound of a thousand crackling potato chip bags. The doctor tries to ask you a thoughtful question. You move an inch. The table screams. You both pretend this isn't happening. By the end of the appointment, you have given the paper the texture of a used origami crane.

→ The paper will be replaced in approximately seven seconds. It took you twelve minutes to destroy it.

Incident № 12 · The Mysterious Third Party

The Fellow in the Corner

At some point a second person enters — young, wearing a white coat, standing quietly near the sink. Nobody explains who they are. You assume they're a medical student or resident. They observe everything with keen interest. They take one note, say nothing, and leave. The doctor doesn't mention them afterward. You never find out their name.

→ You are in their notes. They are not in yours.
✚

Incident № 13 · The Records Problem

"Are You Up to Date on Your Vaccines?"

A simple question. And yet — are you? You think you had a tetanus shot maybe four years ago, or possibly six, or was that the flu shot? Was the flu shot the one that made your arm sore for a week or was that the shingles vaccine your doctor recommended even though you thought that was for people twenty years older than you? You give the extremely confident answer: "I think so?"

→ You are not up to date. You will get one today. Your arm will be sore. You will forget why.

Incident № 14 · The Specialist Pipeline

The Referral Paperwork

The doctor determines you need to see a specialist. She sends a referral. You will now enter a secondary waiting period of between one and eleven weeks, during which you will call the specialist's office twice, be told they're "still processing," call your doctor's office to confirm they sent it, be told they did, call the specialist again, be put on hold for seventeen minutes, and eventually receive an appointment for a date that is technically still within the same calendar year.

→ The appointment is in October. It is currently May.

Incident № 15 · The Inevitable

The Diagnosis That Is Simply "Aging"

You describe a symptom — a creak, a twinge, a mysterious stiffness that wasn't there before. The doctor listens attentively. Examines you. Considers. Then delivers the most unsatisfying verdict in the English language: "Well, as we get older…" The sentence doesn't need to finish. You both know where it's going. You nod. You pay your copay. You go home and research supplements, none of which will help, which is fine.

→ You will take the supplements for six weeks, then forget to reorder them. Nothing will change either way.

Incident № 16 · The Results

The Follow-Up Call That Arrives at the Worst Possible Moment

The office says they'll "call with your results in a few days." Twelve days later, you're in the middle of a tense work meeting when your phone rings from an unknown number. You step out in a mild panic. It's the nurse. Everything is normal. She says "have a nice day" and hangs up. You stand in the hallway for a moment, then go back in and try to remember what the meeting was about.

→ It was about Q3 projections. You will never remember your contribution. Normal.
