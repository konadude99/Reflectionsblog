Everything
They Added
to the Car

From the 1886 Patent-Motorwagen to the car that parks itself, a century of engineers solving problems that drivers didn't yet know they had — and a few they always did.

By Staff · The Peculiar Gazette · Automotive History & Everyday Ingenuity Desk

There is a 1940 Ford F1 pickup truck somewhere in Durango, Colorado — no power steering, no power brakes, no radio, no turn signals, no air conditioning, no lock on the driver's door, a gear shift that requires actual engagement of the human body — and the person who drives it understands something that most drivers have forgotten: that all of those absent things were invented. Someone had to think of them. Someone had to argue for them. Someone had to install the first one and wait to see if the world changed. And then, slowly, it did.

The story of the automobile is not merely a story about faster engines or sleeker bodies. It is a story about the accretion of solutions — each generation inheriting the car its predecessors built, identifying what was still wrong, and going to work on it. The car you drive today is, in ways easy to forget, an extraordinary palimpsest: decades of engineering written over each other, each layer trying to correct the discomforts and dangers of the layer before.

Begin at the beginning, which is less comfortable than you might wish.

The First Era · 1886–1910The Thing That Required Courage

Karl Benz's Patent-Motorwagen of 1886 — generally accepted as the first true automobile — was a three-wheeled contraption with a single-cylinder gasoline engine, no roof, no windshield, no headlamps, and brakes operated by leather pads pressed against the rear wheels by hand. It had a maximum speed of about ten miles per hour, which was probably fast enough to feel life-threatening. There was no reverse gear. You pushed it backward.

Henry Ford's Model T, which arrived in 1908 and transformed the car from novelty to necessity, was a more serious machine but still formidably demanding of its driver. You started it with a hand crank inserted into the front of the engine — a procedure that, if the engine kicked back unexpectedly, could break a wrist or worse. You adjusted ignition timing with a lever. You managed three pedals, two levers, and a throttle, all with different functions from what your hands and feet expected. The roads were largely unpaved. There were no traffic lights, no lane markings, no rules to speak of. Driving in 1908 was an athletic skill learned through exposure to genuine danger.

What early cars did have — by necessity — were speedometers, because you needed to know how fast you were going, and headlamps, because horses had eyes and cars did not. Everything else was the driver's problem.

The 1910s–1920sFirst, Stop Killing People at the Start

The first great quality-of-life improvement for the automobile driver was not comfort. It was the electric starter, and it deserves more gratitude than it typically receives.

Before 1912, starting a car required inserting a crank into the front of the engine and turning it, by force, until the engine fired. The crank had to be gripped in a specific way; if you gripped it wrong and the engine backfired, the crank spun backward and struck whatever was in its path — typically a thumb, a wrist, a forearm. A backfiring crank killed and maimed drivers regularly. It also meant that elderly people, people with limited upper-body strength, and most women simply could not start a car unassisted. The automobile was, quite literally, not universally operable.

In 1912, engineer Charles Kettering introduced the electric starter on the Cadillac Model Thirty. You inserted a key, turned it, and the engine started. The crank handle was relegated to the back of the parts drawer, where it gradually became a curiosity. Within a decade, virtually every American car had abandoned it.

The same decade brought two more things that driving desperately required: hydraulic brakes and the closed body. Before hydraulic brakes, cars stopped using mechanical systems — cables, rods, and levers — that were inconsistent, that pulled to one side, and that required significant physical force to operate. The hydraulic system, patented by Malcolm Loughead in 1918 and first appearing on a production car in 1921, used fluid pressure to apply the brakes smoothly and equally at all four wheels. Stopping became something you could do deliberately rather than hopefully.

The closed body — a roof, windows, doors — sounds obvious in retrospect. It was not obvious in the early years of the automobile, when cars were essentially motorized carriages: open, exposed, and seasonal. The fully enclosed all-steel body became standard by the late 1920s. For the first time, you could drive in the rain without getting wet, and in winter without adequate preparation being a matter of survival.

"Driving in 1908 was an athletic skill learned through exposure to genuine danger. The electric starter, in 1912, was the first quiet revolution — making the car operable by everyone, not merely the strong."

The 1920s–1930sThe Arrival of Pleasure

With basic safety established, engineers began working on comfort and convenience — the things that made driving not just possible, but enjoyable. Windshield wipers had appeared in rudimentary form as early as 1903, operated by hand from inside the car (you cranked a handle to move a blade back and forth, which required taking one hand off the wheel in the rain, which rather negated the purpose). Electric wipers arrived in 1917, automated ones in 1922, and the simple mechanical problem of seeing in the rain was, at last, solved.

In 1930, brothers Paul and Joseph Galvin introduced the Motorola — the first commercially successful car radio, priced at $130 at a time when a complete Ford Model A cost $540. The name "Motorola" was a portmanteau of "motor" and "Victrola," the beloved phonograph brand, chosen to evoke the idea of music in motion. Early adopters had to contend with static from the car's own ignition system, with a separate battery pack under the passenger seat, and with proposed legislation in Massachusetts and Missouri that would have banned car radios entirely on safety grounds. A 1934 poll found that 56% of Americans thought in-car radios were dangerous distractions.

They were not banned. By 1946, nine million American cars had radios. By 1963, more than half of all cars on American roads had them. Today, suggesting their removal would be received as a form of madness.

Turn signals arrived in 1939, introduced by Buick — though the Ford F1 that rolled off the line in 1940 did not yet carry them as standard equipment. The idea was not immediately universal: for years, drivers had communicated their intentions by sticking an arm out the window, and a non-trivial portion of the driving public considered the arm signal perfectly adequate. It was adequate, in the sense that it worked, provided you were not driving in winter, or in rain, or at speed, or at night.

What Your 1940 F1 Was Missing — And What The World Had Just Invented

Your truck rolled off the line the same year the automobile world was in the middle of a transition. Packard had just introduced the first automotive air conditioning system in 1939 — though it was a $274 option on a car that cost about $1,000, and it occupied the entire trunk space. Hydraulic brakes had been standard on most cars for over a decade, but Ford had only just adopted them — in 1939, the year before your truck was built. Turn signals existed, but not yet universally. The radio was ten years old and newly practical.

The F1 you drive represents, in a very real sense, the last moment before the modern car began accumulating its arsenal of amenities. Everything that makes today's car what it is came after — or just barely coincided with — the year your truck was made.

The 1940s–1950sThe Postwar Leap

The Second World War interrupted almost everything, including the development of the civilian automobile. When it ended, the industry came back with remarkable energy, and the late 1940s and 1950s became perhaps the most consequential single decade in the history of automotive comfort and capability.

The automatic transmission arrived at scale. Oldsmobile's Hydra-Matic, introduced in 1940 and spreading rapidly through the 1950s, removed the clutch pedal from the driving experience entirely. Drivers who had spent years learning the art of the manual gear change — the coordination of clutch and throttle, the feel of the synchronizers, the particular embarrassment of stalling on a hill — now simply put the car in Drive and went. Many purists mourned this. Many more people who had never quite mastered the manual transmission quietly rejoiced.

Power steering came in 1951, introduced by Chrysler under the name "Hydraguide." Before it, turning the steering wheel of a large American car at low speed — parking, maneuvering, slow corners — required real physical effort: both hands, some weight behind them, a certain determination. Power steering reduced this effort to almost nothing. By the mid-1950s, it was a must-have feature, and the full-sized American cars of that era — which were growing substantially heavier — became manageable without it only in the way that most things are manageable if you are willing to work hard enough.

Air conditioning returned as a practical option in 1953, courtesy of Chrysler, after Packard's 1939 trunk-filling experiment had faded away. By the late 1960s, more than half of new American cars had it. The effect on long-distance summer driving — on the American family road trip, on the experience of living in the South and Southwest, on the basic quality of July — was not small. Air conditioning did not merely make driving more comfortable. It changed which parts of the country felt livable, and therefore, in time, where people chose to live.

Cruise control arrived in the late 1950s, invented by Ralph Teetor — a blind engineer, which is one of history's richer ironies. Teetor had reportedly been irritated by the way his lawyer's driving sped up and slowed down during conversation, and had resolved to solve the problem mechanically. His speed-control device, first offered as an option in 1958, allowed drivers to set a highway speed and hold it without maintaining pressure on the accelerator. Long-haul driving ceased, for the first time, to require constant physical attention to the throttle.

The 1950s–1960sSafety Becomes Serious

For most of the automobile's first fifty years, safety was largely the driver's concern — a matter of skill, attention, and luck. Cars were designed to carry people and to run; whether those people survived a crash was treated, implicitly, as largely outside the manufacturer's brief. This began to change in the 1950s, slowly and against considerable resistance from an industry that worried, not without reason, that associating automobiles with death might dampen the purchase enthusiasm of consumers.

The three-point seat belt, introduced by Volvo engineer Nils Bohlin in 1959, is the single most consequential safety innovation in the history of the automobile. Volvo's decision to make the patent freely available to every manufacturer — choosing widespread adoption over licensing revenue — may be equally consequential. The three-point belt, which restrained both the lap and the shoulder, was a substantial improvement over earlier two-point lap belts, which in a frontal collision could allow a driver's upper body to swing forward into the steering wheel or dashboard with severe effect. Bohlin's design distributed crash forces across the chest, pelvis, and shoulder — the strongest parts of the human body. By Volvo's own estimate, the belt has saved more than one million lives.

Seat belts, however, faced a remarkable popular resistance. Automakers worried they would remind buyers of accidents. Drivers worried about being trapped in burning cars. Adoption was voluntary and slow. Wisconsin became the first state to require belts be installed in front seats in 1961 — not worn, merely present. Federal law mandating installation nationally came in 1968. Mandatory wearing did not become law in any American state until 1984, in New York. The gap between invention and enforcement was twenty-five years. In those twenty-five years, a great many people died in accidents that a belt would have prevented.

Other safety advances of the era were less contested, because they were less visible. Collapsible steering columns — which telescoped inward in a frontal collision rather than driving the steering wheel into the driver's chest — became federally mandated in 1968. Crumple zones, the deliberate engineering of the car's front and rear sections to absorb impact energy before it reached the passenger compartment, were developed by Mercedes-Benz engineer Bela Barenyi and introduced in 1959. Disc brakes, which provided significantly better stopping power than drum brakes — especially in wet conditions and under repeated hard use — moved from racing cars to production vehicles through the 1960s, becoming standard on most cars by the early 1970s.

"The three-point seat belt was freely given to the entire automobile industry by Volvo in 1959 — a single engineer's invention that has since saved more than one million lives. Its adoption took twenty-five years to become law."

The 1960s–1980sConveniences Become Expectations

Electric windows, which had existed as a luxury feature since the early 1940s, became widely available in the 1960s. So did heated seats — first appearing as an option on Saab vehicles, later spreading. Intermittent windshield wipers were patented in 1969, removing the binary choice between wipers off and wipers thrashing at full speed through drizzle. Power door locks followed, removing the passenger-side ritual of leaning across to unlock the door by hand.

These are not, individually, dramatic advances. But their cumulative effect was a transformation in what driving felt like: less effortful, less physically demanding, more attentive to the driver as a human being rather than a machine operator. The car was becoming a room as much as a vehicle. The expectation of comfort was rising, and it never stopped rising.

Airbags were conceptualized in the early 1950s and experimented with through the 1960s, but proved surprisingly difficult to make practical. The challenge was not the bag itself but the trigger: how to inflate a large bag fast enough to be useful in a crash — a period of milliseconds — without injuring occupants in normal driving. General Motors offered airbags as an option in 1974; they did not become standard equipment in American cars until federal mandates in the late 1980s and early 1990s, and side airbags and curtain airbags arrived through the following decade.

Anti-lock brakes — the system that prevented wheels from locking during hard braking and allowed the driver to steer while stopping — appeared on production cars in 1978, in the Mercedes S-Class. Before ABS, the advice for emergency braking was to pump the brakes by hand to prevent lockup: a technique that required training, cool nerves, and precise timing at the worst possible moment. ABS automated the pump, performing it faster and more accurately than any human hand could manage. The computer was now, in an important sense, a better driver than the driver — at least in that one specific emergency.

The 1980s–2000sThe Computer Takes the Wheel

By the 1980s, the automobile was beginning its transformation from a mechanical object with some electrical components to an electronic system with some mechanical components. The engine control unit — the onboard computer that managed fuel injection, ignition timing, and emissions — made engines cleaner, more efficient, and more reliable than any carburetor ever managed. The check engine light, object of widespread dread and occasional willful ignorance, was the first sign that your car now knew more about its own condition than you did.

Electronic stability control, which could sense and correct a skid faster than any driver's reflexes, was introduced in the mid-1990s. Traction control prevented wheel spin on slippery surfaces. GPS navigation began appearing in production cars in the late 1990s, ending the decades-long tradition of pulled-over map consultations and wrong turns taken with confidence. The backup camera — first used on a concept car in 1956, first in a production car in Japan in 1987, eventually federally mandated for all new American cars in 2018 — reversed the driver's blindness to what lay behind them.

Bluetooth connectivity allowed drivers to make phone calls and play music without taking their hands from the wheel. Parking sensors beeped their way toward obstacles. Keyless entry removed the need to locate the key in a pocket or purse. Push-button ignition replaced the turned key. Every small friction of the driving experience was identified and engineered away, one by one, over decades.

The PresentToward the Car That Does Not Need You

Modern vehicles offer lane departure warnings that buzz or tug when the car drifts from its lane. Adaptive cruise control adjusts speed automatically to maintain following distance. Automatic emergency braking can apply the brakes faster than a driver can react when the car detects an impending collision. Some current systems can take over steering, acceleration, and braking entirely — not in all conditions, not yet reliably in all situations, but in enough that the question has ceased to be whether cars will drive themselves and has become when.

The safety numbers are striking. Traffic fatalities in the United States peaked in 1972 at roughly 55,000 per year. By 2022, with far more cars and far more miles driven, the number was around 43,000 — still too many, but 20% lower in absolute terms despite a much larger driving population. Every percentage of that reduction corresponds to a specific technology: to a belt that held, to a bag that inflated, to a brake that caught, to an algorithm that corrected the physics of a skid before the driver knew it had started.

None of this happened automatically. Each feature was designed, tested, argued over, initially optional, eventually standard, ultimately invisible. The backup camera did not feel like a profound safety advance when it became mandatory. It felt like something that had always been there. This is how progress works: the generation that knew the absence marvels; the next takes for granted.

The Absent Things

What a 1940 Ford F1 does not have is, in its way, a catalog of the twentieth century's engineering preoccupations. No power steering: the road pushes back through your hands, and you feel the weight of the vehicle in every turn. No power brakes: stopping requires real force, deliberate effort — a physical negotiation between driver and machine. No air conditioning: you drive in the weather, and the weather is part of the experience. No radio: the silence is the sound of the engine and the wind. No turn signals: the arm goes out the window, which is honest about the fact that you are about to do something.

There is, in all of this, a kind of clarity. The car demands your attention because it cannot function without it. You are not a passenger who occasionally steers. You are a participant. The machine makes nothing easy, which means that when you bring it home, you have earned something. Whether that is worth the inconvenience is a question only the driver can answer. Many who drive these trucks say it is.

A century and a quarter of automobile development has produced vehicles of extraordinary capability and comfort — cars that know when they are drifting, that stop themselves, that navigate, that remember your preferred seat position and climate settings and radio stations. It has also produced a generation of drivers who have never felt what it is to muscle a car through a turn without power assist, to pump brakes on ice, or to read a paper map in a gas station parking lot while someone in the passenger seat maintains an unhelpful silence.

Neither world is wholly better than the other. What the 1940 F1 offers that the modern car cannot is a particular kind of honesty — the honest communication of a machine that does not manage its own operation, that tells you through your hands and feet and spine exactly what it is doing and exactly what it requires. Modern cars are safer, more comfortable, more capable, and more forgiving. They are also, in some ways, less known to the people who drive them.

That is the trade. Most people take it gladly. Some few keep a 1940 truck in the garage, just to remember what driving once required, and what it once felt like to be the whole of the machine.
