A Century of Childhood · 1906 — 2026

What Every
10-Year-Old
Knew

A whimsical, completely unfair, and historically fascinating tour through 120 years of childhood skills — from milking cows and memorizing Morse code to building Minecraft cities and explaining TikTok to confused adults.

↓ scroll through time ↓

The Edwardian Age

1906

The Ten-Year-Old Who Could Run a Farm
and Probably Already Was

In 1906, approximately 60% of American children lived on farms. A ten-year-old wasn't a child in the modern sense — they were a small, underpaid farmhand with homework. Before school (which started at a brisk walk away, usually across fields), they'd already milked a cow, fed the chickens, and quite possibly made a decision about a pig. The "childhood" portion of the day was largely squeezed between chores, more chores, and falling asleep while technically still doing chores.

"When I was ten, I already knew how to harness a horse, split wood, and deliver a calf. I also knew that complaining about it was not an option." — Basically every grandfather ever.
🐄
Animal Husbandry
Milk a cow by hand, identify which chicken was "off," determine which pig would not be attending Christmas. Essential survival skills for most American children.
🪓
Wood Splitting & Fire-Building
Not as a fun outdoor activity. As the way the house stays warm and the food gets cooked. Before breakfast, every day, without being asked.
✍️
Copperplate Cursive
The Palmer Method was mandatory. Your penmanship was a moral matter. Sloppy handwriting suggested weak character. Children practiced loops for hours.
🌾
Crop Knowledge
Which seeds go in when. What bad soil looks like. How to tell if wheat is ready. Not trivia — this was knowing whether the family eats this winter.
🧵
Mending & Basic Sewing
A torn sock gets mended, not replaced. New clothes arrive once a year if you're lucky. A 10-year-old could darn, patch, and reattach buttons without thinking about it.
📐
Mental Arithmetic
No calculators. No phones. The price of feed, the count of bushels, the change from a dollar — all computed in your head, and you'd better be right.

The Great War Era

1916

The Ten-Year-Old Who Grew the Food
While Dad Was Overseas

By 1916, World War I was consuming fathers and older brothers, and the home front needed everyone. Ten-year-olds were growing Victory Gardens, learning to preserve food in jars (a matter of survival, not Pinterest), and delivering messages on foot because the telephone was a novelty and telegrams cost money. They also knew — probably better than most adults today — the exact difference between a turnip and a rutabaga, because this knowledge could save the family dinner.

A 1916 ten-year-old could butcher a chicken for Sunday supper. A 2016 ten-year-old cannot locate Sunday supper on a menu without photos.
🫙
Food Preservation
Canning, pickling, drying. If you didn't put food up in summer, you didn't eat in winter. A ten-year-old could safely process a batch of green beans or jam without supervision.
🌿
Victory Garden Management
With food rationed and men away, children actually ran vegetable plots. They knew planting schedules, companion planting, and how to keep aphids off the tomatoes.
📬
Message Running & Errand Logic
The original human router. Delivered messages, picked up goods from multiple shops in efficient order, and remembered everything because writing it down was optional.
🧼
Soap and Candle Making
Still common enough that a child might help render lard and lye into soap bars. Ugly. Effective. Your hands smelled of it all week.
📰
Reading a Newspaper Properly
Not skimming. Reading — understanding the war dispatches, the casualty lists, the rationing announcements. Ten-year-olds were expected to follow events seriously.
🎵
Parlour Music
Piano, fiddle, or singing — the household entertainment before radio. Children played for family gatherings, and practice was not optional. Three hymns minimum.

The Roaring Twenties

1926

The Ten-Year-Old Who Tuned the Radio
and Did the Charleston in the Kitchen

The 1920s brought electricity to cities, the automobile to the middle class, and — most critically to a ten-year-old — the radio. For the first time, children could access entertainment that nobody had pre-approved. Jazz music drifted in from New York and Chicago. Baseball games were announced live. A ten-year-old in 1926 knew how to operate, tune, and troubleshoot a crystal radio set, and considered this essentially the coolest skill a person could possess.

A 1926 ten-year-old had memorized every baseball statistic, could Charlston passably, and knew Prohibition was nonsense because they'd seen the speakeasy on Third Street. They said nothing about it.
📻
Radio Operation & Tuning
Crystal radio sets required real skill — finding the signal, adjusting the cat's whisker, fighting interference. A child who could coax a clear signal was a household hero.
🚗
Basic Auto Mechanics
Cars broke down constantly. A ten-year-old could hand Father the right wrench, understood what a spark plug was, and could crank-start a Model T in a pinch.
⚾
Box Scores & Sports Statistics
Could read a newspaper box score, knew every player's batting average, and could argue Babe Ruth's stats with the authority of a small, opinionated professor.
💃
Social Dancing
The Charleston, the Foxtrot. Dancing wasn't optional — it was the social currency at every party, school event, and church social. You learned or you stood against the wall.
🧮
Double-Entry Bookkeeping
Many children helped maintain the family shop's ledger by age 10. Arithmetic wasn't abstract — it was the margin between profit and ruin, and you learned to respect it.
🎭
Elocution & Recitation
Memorizing and dramatically performing poems and speeches before an audience was a standard school skill. "The boy stood on the burning deck" — delivered with feeling.

The Great Depression

1936

The Ten-Year-Old Who Knew Exactly
How Little Was in the Tin

Grew up during the Depression — one of the toughest generations of childhoods in American history. In 1936, a ten-year-old understood the value of a meal in a way that would be difficult to explain to any child born after 1950. They knew how to make something from nothing: flour-sack clothes, games with sticks, entertainment from a battery-powered radio shared between three families on the block. They were experts at the skills of creative scarcity — the lost art of making do.

A 1936 ten-year-old could stretch a pot of soup to feed eight people, recite FDR's Fireside Chats from memory, and had absolutely zero patience for waste. They still don't. Check their refrigerator — nothing gets thrown out.
1936 kid's toyA stick. And a smaller stick for comparison.
VS
2026 kid's toyA $500 device running 60fps at 4K resolution.
♻️
Radical Repurposing
Flour sacks became shirts. Worn tires became shoe soles. Tin cans became cups. Nothing was thrown away without first asking if it could be something else.
🪙
Depression-Era Budgeting
Every coin was accounted for. A ten-year-old could stretch a meal's ingredients across multiple days and understand instinctively why this mattered.
🎙️
Radio Listening (Deeply)
One radio in the neighborhood, shared. Children listened to FDR's Fireside Chats, Jack Benny, and Lone Ranger serials — and remembered every word. No rewind.
🤝
Barter & Trade
Cash was scarce, so goods were exchanged. A ten-year-old understood the relative value of two dozen eggs vs. a pound of flour, and negotiated seriously.
🌿
Foraging & Wild Foods
Dandelion greens, wild berries, hickory nuts. Kids in rural areas knew which plants were edible and how to prepare them — not as wilderness survival but as dinner.
🎭
Making Your Own Fun
No purchased entertainment. Marbles, mumbletypeg, kick-the-can, elaborate games invented from nothing. The imagination was the only device with unlimited battery.

Postwar Boom

1946

The Ten-Year-Old Who Roamed Free,
Built Things, and Came Home at Dark

The war was over. Dad was home. Suburbia was being built with astonishing speed, and with it came a new kind of childhood: free-range, unsupervised, and genuinely dangerous in delightful ways. A ten-year-old in 1946 left the house in the morning and returned — possibly with blood on their knee — at dinner. In between, they organized their own games, built their own forts, settled their own disputes, and generally operated as a small society of people who had never heard the word "playdate."

A 1946 ten-year-old could build a go-kart from salvaged parts, navigate home from three miles away using only the sun, and had almost certainly broken at least one bone that nobody went to the doctor about.
🚲
Bicycle Maintenance
Fix a flat, adjust brakes, oil a chain. The bike was freedom, and keeping it running was a serious responsibility. Taking it to a shop was not an option that occurred to anyone.
🏡
Fort Architecture
Multi-room tree forts, underground clubhouses, elaborate systems of salvaged lumber. Engineering without an engineer, construction without a permit, and inspection by nobody.
⚾
Sandlot Sports Self-Organization
No adults, no uniforms, no refs. Kids chose teams, set rules, resolved disputes, and played for hours. The social skills this required were, frankly, remarkable.
🔧
Basic Construction & Repair
Hammering, sawing, basic plumbing assistance. Boys especially were expected to hand tools, help with repairs, and understand how a house basically worked.
📺
Early TV Literacy
By 1946-50, TV was arriving. A ten-year-old could adjust the rabbit ears, explain why the picture went snowy, and had strong opinions about Howdy Doody.
🗺️
Navigating Without Help
Three miles from home, no phone, no map, no worry. Landmarks, sun position, and the general knowledge of the neighborhood got you home. Always.

The Atomic Fifties

1956

The Ten-Year-Old Who Knew Every
Elvis Lyric and Did Duck-and-Cover

The fifties were a study in contradiction for a child: relentless optimism and nuclear anxiety, all in the same week. Your teacher taught you to duck under your desk in the event of atomic attack (which, everyone privately agreed, would help exactly no one). Meanwhile, television was rewriting childhood entirely — suddenly there was something to watch, and watching it together was the new family activity. A ten-year-old in 1956 could tell you the plot of every I Love Lucy episode, had a strong opinion about Elvis, and could do a paper route blindfolded.

A 1956 ten-year-old had memorized "Hound Dog," understood the offside rule in football, and had a very specific plan for surviving nuclear war that began with "get under the desk" and became increasingly vague thereafter.
📰
Paper Route Logistics
Up at 5am, fold, rubber-band, deliver 80+ papers across a half-mile route in rain, snow, or darkness — then collect money door-to-door on Fridays. A masterclass in small business.
🎵
45rpm Record Operation
The yellow plastic insert, the needle placement, keeping your older sibling's records unscratched — a complex ritual for listening to Elvis without getting killed.
📺
TV Rabbit Ear Mastery
Standing completely still, arm extended at 38°, holding the antenna in the exact position that produced a non-snowy picture. The family's human tripod.
🧠
Encyclopaedia Research
The family encyclopaedia set — probably Britannica or World Book — was the research tool. You knew which volume had which letter and how to use the index. No shortcuts.
🚘
Car Culture Knowledge
Every ten-year-old boy could identify any car by silhouette, knew the horsepower of each model, and had strong opinions about whether fins were improving or getting ridiculous.
🧹
Full Household Chores
Vacuuming, dishes, yard work, laundry assistance. Chores weren't negotiated — they were assigned and completed. Allowance was not guaranteed. Character was assumed to be the reward.

The Space Age Sixties

1966

The Ten-Year-Old Who Was Going to
Be an Astronaut and Meant It

In 1966, the space race was at full throttle, and every child fully expected to visit the moon personally within the next fifteen years. This was not naive — it felt inevitable. The cultural atmosphere was electric: the Beatles on the transistor radio, Gemini missions on black-and-white TV, and a peculiar national optimism that anything was achievable if you applied enough math. Ten-year-olds were the first generation who grew up expecting technology to solve everything. The first in history to be wrong about that in a specific, Saturn-V-shaped way.

A 1966 ten-year-old could name every Mercury and Gemini astronaut, every Beatle's real name, and was deeply, personally offended by the cancellation of Lost in Space. These feelings have never fully resolved.
🚀
Space Mission Knowledge
Every astronaut, every mission, the differences between Mercury, Gemini, and Apollo. This was not trivia — this was the most important thing happening on Earth and children took it seriously.
📻
Transistor Radio Literacy
The pocket radio was the first truly personal entertainment device. A ten-year-old knew every top-40 station, could identify songs in three notes, and owned their musical taste completely.
🧪
Model Rocketry & Chemistry Sets
Estes rockets launched from fields. Chemistry sets with actual chemicals. Experiments that occasionally produced smoke. Safety culture was aspirational rather than enforced.
🎸
Pop Music Obsession
The Beatles, The Stones, Motown. A ten-year-old in 1966 was navigating the most exciting music moment in American history and had very strong opinions about which Beatle was best.
🗞️
Understanding the News (Grimly)
Vietnam. Civil Rights. Assassinations. Sixties children processed world events at a level unusual for their age. History was happening loudly, and they were watching.
🎨
Hobby Crafts & Model Building
Airfix model kits, paint-by-numbers, stamp collecting. The concept of absorbing yourself in a solitary analog project for four hours straight. A lost civilization.

The Bicentennial Seventies

1976

The Ten-Year-Old Who Skateboarded
Without Pads and Survived Star Wars

1976 was America's Bicentennial, and children celebrated by largely ignoring it in favor of skateboarding, Dungeons & Dragons, and waiting for Star Wars to come out (one more year). This was the decade of unsupervised outdoor freedom reaching its absolute peak — children genuinely roamed in packs without adult contact for entire days, and this was considered fine. The decade also brought the Pet Rock, which suggests the nation's sense of humor was in excellent shape.

A 1976 ten-year-old could ollie off a curb, understood the difference between a paladin and a ranger, had a Pet Rock they took very seriously for approximately ten days, and could explain to you in great detail why Star Wars was going to be amazing — nine months before it came out.
🛹
Skateboarding
Without helmets, pads, or instruction videos. You learned by falling. Repeatedly. The falling was considered part of the learning, not a design flaw requiring a lawsuit.
🎲
Dungeons & Dragons
D&D launched in 1974 and by 1976 a ten-year-old could run a multi-session campaign, calculate THAC0, and explain to their alarmed parents that no, it was not Satanic.
🎞️
Movie Obsession & Memorization
Jaws (1975), Rocky (1976). Every scene memorized. Movies were events — you saw them in theaters multiple times, then waited years for any chance to see them again.
🎶
Making Mix Tapes from Radio
Finger on the Record button, listening for the exact moment your song started, trying not to catch the DJ's voice. An art form requiring patience, reflexes, and tape.
🌳
Unsupervised Wilderness Competence
Woods, creeks, construction sites, abandoned lots. Ten-year-olds assessed their own risk, made their own decisions, and turned up for dinner. Usually.
🕹️
Early Arcade Gaming
Pong. Space Invaders. Atari arriving at home. The earliest generation of video game literacy — understanding that you could control something on a screen was still new and wonderful.

The Digital Dawn

1986

THE TEN-YEAR-OLD WHO TYPED
BASIC INTO A COMPUTER AND
SAVED IT TO A FLOPPY DISK_

The personal computer arrived in homes, and the first generation of truly digital children was born. They typed 10 PRINT "HELLO"; 20 GOTO 10 into Apple IIs in school computer labs and felt like hackers. They also had the NES, which introduced a level of game complexity that required actual strategy. Meanwhile, the Walkman meant music was finally personal — no more negotiating with siblings over the record player. A ten-year-old in 1986 lived simultaneously in two worlds: the analog one that had always existed, and the digital one just flickering to life.

A 1986 ten-year-old could type a BASIC program, rewind a VHS tape before returning it, defeat the original Legend of Zelda without a walkthrough, and explain to their father what a floppy disk was. The father nodded slowly. He understood almost none of it.
1986 storage360KB floppy disk. Treated like a precious artifact.
VS
2026 storage2TB phone. Mostly TikTok drafts and screenshots.
💾
BASIC Programming
Apple II BASIC. Writing your name in a loop. Making the cursor blink. This was it — the beginning of everything. A ten-year-old who could do this felt genuinely powerful.
🕹️
Console Gaming Mastery
NES, Atari 7800, Commodore 64. Gaming that required memorization, pattern recognition, and persistence. No save states. No tutorials. Figure it out or quit forever.
📼
VHS Operation & Etiquette
Be kind, rewind. Knowing which speed recorded how long. The art of hitting record during a movie broadcast and hoping the tape didn't run out before the ending.
🎧
Walkman Management
Which side of the tape you were on. When batteries were dying (the pitch drop). The complex social question of whether to offer one earbud and to whom.
📞
Telephone Protocol
"Hello, may I speak to [friend]?" — and if their parent answered, the formal pleasantries required. The social choreography of the family phone line was precise.
🗺️
Paper Map Reading
The folded road atlas in the glove box. Tracking the route with your finger. Understanding scale. Knowing which highway connects where. Pre-GPS navigation was a real skill.

The Internet Arrives

1996

The Ten-Year-Old Who Waited 45 Minutes
for One JPEG to Load and Was Fine With It

The internet came home, and it sounded like a robot having a breakdown. Dial-up modems connected at 28.8 kbps — the speed of geological processes — and a ten-year-old in 1996 would sit and watch a single image load one pixel row at a time, and this was genuinely exciting. They had a Tamagotchi (which they kept alive with the intensity of new parents), an AOL screen name with exactly the right amount of random numbers in it, and could type faster than most adults using only their thumbs on a keyboard. The world was changing fast and they were changing with it.

"You've got mail!" — three words that made a 1996 ten-year-old drop everything and sprint to the computer, then discover it was a forward from Grandma about angels. Disappointment managed. Tamagotchi checked. Hunger: 3 hearts.
🐣
Tamagotchi Management
Feeding, cleaning, sleeping schedules. The first widely distributed experience of digital responsibility. Children took this seriously. The Tamagotchi dying was a genuine emotional event.
💬
AIM / Online Chat Etiquette
The away message as literary form. The buddy list as social map. The art of the screen name. Internet communication had its own rules and children mastered them before adults noticed.
🎮
CD-ROM Gaming
Myst. Oregon Trail. Where in the World Is Carmen Sandiego. Games that required reading, problem-solving, and navigating menus. The educational game was peak 90s.
📀
CD Care & Handling
Never touch the shiny side. Wipe from center outward. Understanding that a scratch could corrupt everything. The physical media era required physical responsibility.
🌐
Basic Web Navigation
Typing a URL in Netscape. Understanding that "www" was required. Knowing what a search engine was before Google. The early internet was navigated, not searched.
☎️
Not Tying Up the Phone Line
Dial-up and the landline competed for the same wire. A ten-year-old managed this social complexity with the authority of a small telecommunications regulator.

The Social Media Dawn

2006

The Ten-Year-Old Who Had a MySpace Page
With an Autoplay Song Nobody Asked For

2006 was the last year of relatively innocent internet childhoods — right before smartphones changed everything forever. A ten-year-old had an iPod Nano they treated like a religious artifact, a growing awareness of YouTube (launched 2005), and possibly a MySpace page with a carefully selected song that would blast at full volume when any visitor arrived. They texted on a flip phone by pressing the number buttons multiple times — 7-7-7 for S, 2-2-2 for C — with the speed and accuracy of a telegraph operator. This was considered completely normal.

A 2006 ten-year-old could customize their MySpace page with HTML they'd copied from a tutorial, had a "Top 8" that was a source of significant social anxiety, and could T9-text 40 words per minute without looking down. They also had an iPod with 500 songs on it and felt profoundly wealthy.
🎵
iPod/iTunes Library Management
Ripping CDs. Creating playlists. The careful curation of 1000 songs. Organizing by genre, mood, activity. The first generation of personal algorithmic music taste.
📱
T9 Texting at Speed
Multi-tap texting on a numeric keypad, at competitive speeds, with predictive text turned on or off based on preference. A fine motor skill now completely extinct.
🌐
Basic HTML / MySpace Customization
Copy-pasting CSS to change profile colors. Embedding music. Adding glitter GIFs. The entry point to web development for an entire generation, accidentally.
🎮
Console Gaming Strategy
PS2, GameCube, early Xbox 360. Games with complex narratives and online multiplayer arriving. A ten-year-old navigated virtual worlds with the confidence of a city planner.
📺
YouTube Discovery
Finding and sharing videos — "Charlie Bit My Finger" was peak 2007 culture. Understanding that anyone could post video online was genuinely mind-expanding.
🔍
Google-Fu
Effective searching. Knowing to add "-" to exclude terms, using quotes for exact phrases. The early internet rewarded those who could search precisely.

The App Generation

2016

The Ten-Year-Old Who Built an Entire City
in Minecraft and Runs a YouTube Channel

By 2016, the smartphone had been in a ten-year-old's life since they were three, which meant they had always known touchscreens and found a laptop keyboard slightly confusing and old-fashioned. They had Minecraft cities of extraordinary ambition — elaborate redstone circuits, multi-building districts, transportation networks. They were also the first generation for whom creating online content felt natural: recording, editing, and posting video was something you just did, in the same way a previous generation just drew pictures.

A 2016 ten-year-old could edit a YouTube video, build a working calculator in Minecraft using redstone logic gates, navigate Snapchat's features faster than any adult alive, and explain to their parents with gentle, patient condescension how to fix their phone settings. Every time.
1956 kid's entertainmentOne TV channel. Watch it or don't.
VS
2016 kid's entertainmentInfinite content. Nothing to watch.
⛏️
Minecraft World-Building
Architecture, resource management, redstone engineering, server navigation. A ten-year-old's Minecraft world was often more structurally ambitious than most adults' actual renovation plans.
🎥
Basic Video Editing
iMovie, basic YouTube production. Recording, trimming, adding music, uploading. A ten-year-old treated content creation as a normal creative outlet, not a career.
📲
Multi-Platform Social Navigation
Instagram, Snapchat, YouTube, each with different cultures, norms, and audiences. Managing a presence across platforms — even a small one — required genuine social intelligence.
🎮
Fortnite / Roblox Mastery
Not just playing — understanding the mechanics, the meta, the economy (V-Bucks, Robux), the social hierarchy. Gaming had become its own complete social world.
📱
Touchscreen as Primary Interface
Born into touchscreens, a 2016 ten-year-old navigated apps, settings, and interfaces instinctively — then had to consciously learn how a mouse worked in school.
🧩
Troubleshooting Family Tech
The de facto family IT department. Fixing the WiFi, setting up the streaming box, explaining why the printer doesn't work. A thankless but essential role.

The AI Age

2026

The Ten-Year-Old Who Uses AI
as Naturally as a Pencil

A ten-year-old in 2026 has never known a world without smartphones, streaming, or touchscreens. They are growing up with AI assistants as a basic tool — using them for homework, creative projects, and answering questions the way a previous generation used Google, and a generation before that used an encyclopaedia. They make TikToks with the unselfconscious fluency of someone for whom the camera has always existed. They are also, quietly, more aware of climate and global systems than any previous childhood generation — because the world has been explaining its predicament to them since they could watch YouTube.

A 2026 ten-year-old can prompt an AI, edit video on a phone in three minutes, build in Roblox with code they wrote themselves, explain carbon footprints clearly, and look at you with the gentle pity of someone who watched you try to screenshot something using entirely the wrong gesture.
1906 ten-year-oldCould operate a farm, harness a horse, and preserve food for winter.
VS
2026 ten-year-oldCan prompt an AI, edit video, code in Scratch, and explain the metaverse.
🤖
AI Prompting & Literacy
Knowing how to ask an AI a useful question — with context, constraints, and iteration. A skill that didn't exist five years ago and is now as natural as using a search engine.
🎵
TikTok Creation & Trend Literacy
Knowing the audio, the format, the timing, the sound. Making a TikTok that performs requires genuine cultural intelligence — reading the room at algorithmic speed.
🌍
Climate & Systems Thinking
Carbon footprints, renewable energy, food systems. Not as abstract environmental concepts but as real frameworks for thinking about daily choices. A fundamentally different worldview.
💻
Coding Basics (Scratch / Python)
Scratch from age 7, Python beginning at 9-10. A ten-year-old who codes today is not unusual — it's expected. Programming literacy is joining reading literacy as a baseline skill.
🧠
Information Verification
A skill previous generations didn't need: figuring out if something is true. Identifying AI-generated images, checking sources, recognizing misinformation. The new critical thinking.
📱
Multi-Device Fluency
Phone, tablet, laptop, smart TV, game console, smart speaker — each with different interfaces, each used naturally. A 2026 ten-year-old manages more devices than a 1986 office.
What they cannot do, and probably should learn: read a paper map, write in cursive, explain what a fax machine is, operate a rotary dial, or start a fire without a YouTube tutorial. The circle of life continues. 🔄

Same Kid. Different Century.
Same Look of Utter Confidence.

What's remarkable about this tour through 120 years of childhood is not how different the skills are — though they are remarkably, entertainingly different — but how constant the underlying thing is. Every ten-year-old, in every era, was an expert in the world they were actually living in. They knew the tools, the rules, the culture, the technology. They could do things adults found baffling. They explained things with patient condescension to the people who were supposed to be teaching them.

The 1906 ten-year-old who could milk a cow and split wood before breakfast would find the 2026 ten-year-old's skills as bewildering as a foreign language. And the 2026 ten-year-old, handed a pail and pointed toward a cow, would produce an expression of pure philosophical despair.

Neither of them is wrong. Both of them are entirely competent in the world they actually inhabit. And both of them, given fifteen minutes of instruction, would probably figure out the other's job — because ten-year-olds have always been, at their core, surprisingly capable people who are very annoyed that nobody seems to realize this.

The cows, for what it's worth, have remained equally unimpressed throughout.
