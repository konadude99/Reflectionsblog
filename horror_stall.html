WHY EVERY PUBLIC RESTROOM
HAS ONE STALL
FROM A HORROR MOVIE

A rigorous investigation into the universal law that governs public restrooms — why they are the way they are, who is responsible, and why nobody will fix the lock on stall three.

By Our Correspondent, Who Has Seen Things  ·  Dept. of Facilities and Dread  ·  Inspection Date: Ongoing  ·  Results: Inconclusive

Exhibit A  ·  Location Withheld  ·  Status: Technically Open  ·  Lock: Not Functional  ·  Last Serviced: Unknown  ·  Do Not Enter

Every public restroom in America contains one stall that should not exist. Not in the sense that it is broken, though it is broken. Not in the sense that the lock doesn't work, though the lock doesn't work. It should not exist in the deeper sense that its presence raises questions about the nature of maintenance, institutional responsibility, and what exactly happened in there that the cleaning crew simply decided to work around rather than address. You know the stall. You have always known the stall.

The other stalls are fine. The other stalls are normal, utilitarian, marginally acceptable. They have functional locks and toilet paper that is at least present, if oriented incorrectly. They are, in the context of a public restroom, unremarkable. And then there is the last stall, or sometimes the first stall, or occasionally the one that is inexplicably set six inches lower than the others, and it is as though the building itself has a room it doesn't want you to go into.

This piece is an attempt to explain why.

The Architecture of DreadThe Design Problem No One Designed Around

Public restrooms in America are built to code. This is the first thing to understand. They are not built to be pleasant, or comfortable, or to inspire confidence in humanity. They are built to meet minimum legal requirements, approved by a committee, constructed by the lowest qualified bidder, and then handed to a facilities department that has other problems. The horror is not accidental. The horror is the result of a rational process operating at its logical conclusion.

Consider the stall door gap. American public restroom stalls have a gap at the bottom — typically six to twelve inches of open air between the door and the floor. This is a design choice. European stalls, by contrast, frequently go floor-to-ceiling, providing something approaching genuine privacy. The American gap exists for reasons that facilities engineers have cited variously as: ventilation, ease of cleaning, visual confirmation that a stall is occupied, and discouraging loitering. These are legitimate operational concerns. They are also the precise explanation for why every person who has ever used a public restroom has made eye contact, through a stall gap, with a stranger, and both parties have had to carry that experience home with them.

The flickering light is a separate matter. Fluorescent tube lighting in institutional settings operates on a ballast — an electrical component that regulates current to the bulb. When the ballast begins to fail, the light flickers. The fix is straightforward and inexpensive: replace the ballast. The reason this is not done is not technical. The reason is that replacing the ballast requires submitting a maintenance request to the facilities department, which goes into a queue behind more pressing concerns, where it will remain until the light fails entirely, at which point a different maintenance request will be filed for the light that is now fully out, and this request will take approximately the same amount of time to process. The flicker is not atmosphere. The flicker is bureaucracy made visible.

"Nobody designed the horror stall. Nobody approved it, permitted it, or intended it. It emerged, the way most institutional failures emerge — through the slow accumulation of deferred decisions, expiring warranties, and a cleaning contract that covers the floor but not the ceiling."

The Contractual HorrorResponsibility, and the Art of Not Having It

The single most important fact about a public restroom is this: the entity responsible for cleaning it is almost certainly different from the entity responsible for repairing it, which is different from the entity that owns the building, which may or may not be the same entity that contracted the original construction, whose warranty has expired. This is not a conspiracy. This is facilities management.

The cleaning contractor comes in on a schedule. Their contract specifies what they are responsible for: mopping the floor, restocking paper products, wiping down surfaces. It does not specify the broken toilet paper dispenser that has been hanging by one bolt for fourteen months, because that is a repair, not a cleaning, and repairs go through a different contract. The repair contractor responds to submitted work orders. The work order for the dispenser was submitted, assigned a ticket number, and entered a queue that is currently backlogged. Nobody is being negligent. Everyone is following their contract. The dispenser hangs by its bolt. This is the system working as designed.

The horror stall, then, is typically not a stall that has been neglected so much as a stall that has fallen between the jurisdictions of everyone responsible for it. The lock is a repair. The stain on the ceiling is unclear — is that a cleaning issue or a structural issue? The door that swings open on its own is probably a hinge, which is maintenance, but the hinge was damaged when someone kicked the door, which might be an insurance matter. Meanwhile, stall three continues to exist. People continue to open it, assess the situation, and close it again.

Behavioral ScienceThe Stall Selection Problem

Researchers — real researchers, at actual universities — have studied which bathroom stalls people choose and why. The findings are, as a body of work, more interesting than they have any right to be.

The consensus finding is that the middle stall gets the most use. People entering a restroom tend to choose a stall that is neither the first nor the last, a behavior attributed to a desire to avoid the extremes — the first stall is too exposed, too close to the entrance; the last stall carries its own associations, particularly in a facility where the last stall is notably worse than the others, which it almost always is. People learn this. They develop heuristics. They walk past stall three without looking directly at it.

This creates a feedback loop with unpleasant implications. The middle stalls, used most heavily, require the most cleaning and the most frequent restocking. If cleaning schedules are fixed rather than demand-responsive — and they almost always are — the middle stalls are consistently the most depleted. The first stall, avoided by people who have read the research, is statistically the best-supplied stall in any public restroom. This information is not widely distributed. The people who know it tend to not mention it in polite conversation because the explanation requires them to discuss their bathroom research in more detail than most social situations permit.

The horror stall, meanwhile, gets less traffic precisely because it is worse, which means it receives less cleaning attention because cleaning is often allocated proportionally to use, which means it gets worse, which means it gets less traffic. The horror stall is a stable equilibrium. It maintains itself through a cycle of mutual avoidance that nobody manages but everyone sustains.

The Rest StopA Special Case

The American highway rest stop restroom deserves its own classification, separate from all other public restroom categories. It is not worse because it is neglected — some are maintained with admirable regularity — but because it exists at the precise intersection of high volume, transient population, remote location, and architectural decisions made in the 1970s that nobody has since had the budget to revisit.

The rest stop restroom is the one public facility you will enter in genuine desperation, having passed the previous rest stop forty miles ago with the intention of holding out for a better option, and having concluded that no better option will arrive. It has a specific smell that is the smell of all rest stop restrooms everywhere, a regional invariant that defies explanation given that each facility is individually maintained. There is always a paper towel dispenser that is either empty or so full it will not dispense. There is always one stall that is out of order, identified by a sign that has been there long enough to have faded. There is always the ambient sound of the highway, which is oddly more audible inside than out.

The rest stop restroom does not have a horror stall so much as it is, in its entirety, the horror stall, with a small number of acceptable stalls distributed within it for contrast.

Field Note  ·  The Sensory Record

What Your Body Registers Before Your Brain Catches Up

The lighting in a public restroom is almost never comfortable. Institutional fluorescent fixtures render everything in a frequency that is simultaneously too bright and too cold, casting a light that makes healthy people look like they need to sit down. In the horror stall, the fixture is flickering, broken, or absent. The effect is not an accident of neglect. It is what happens when a lighting specification written for a budget is installed by a contractor who met the spec and nothing more.

The sound is its own problem. Public restrooms are hard surfaces — tile, porcelain, metal partitions — which means every sound bounces. The hand dryer, a device that exists to reduce paper waste and has been installed in every public restroom since the early 2000s, produces approximately 80 decibels of noise at close range. The Dyson Airblade produces more. These numbers approach the threshold at which OSHA requires hearing protection in a workplace. The hand dryer runs for thirty seconds. This is considered acceptable.

The gap at the bottom of the stall door is, in most facilities, between six and twelve inches. This is enough space to see feet. It is enough space to be seen. It is not enough space to do anything about either of these facts. The gap exists, as noted, for ventilation and cleaning purposes. It persists because changing it would require replacing the partitions, which are structurally integral to the stall system, which would require a facilities upgrade, which requires a budget allocation. The gap remains. Eye contact continues to occur.

The temperature in a public restroom is always wrong. In winter, the room is cold in the specific way of a space that is heated as an afterthought. In summer, it retains heat with the efficiency of a structure that was not designed for human comfort. The horror stall is always the coldest in winter and the most oppressive in summer. This has not been formally studied. It does not need to be.

ConclusionOn the Horror Stall and What It Represents

The horror stall is not a mystery. Once you understand how public facilities are designed, funded, contracted, cleaned, maintained, and inspected, the horror stall is entirely predictable — an inevitable output of a system that distributes responsibility carefully enough that no single party is accountable for the whole. Nobody built the horror stall. Nobody approved it. Nobody is, strictly speaking, responsible for it. It exists in the gaps between contracts, in the queue of outstanding work orders, in the jurisdiction that is perpetually under review.

It is, in this sense, not unique to restrooms. The horror stall is the physical manifestation of a principle that governs most large institutional systems: that a chain of individually reasonable decisions can produce a collectively unreasonable result, and that when it does, the result persists because the system has no mechanism for looking at it whole.

The lock has been broken since February. The stain on the ceiling predates the current facilities manager. The out-of-order sign is laminated, which means someone spent money on it, which means the out-of-order condition was expected to be long-term. Stall three is still there. The work order is still open. The cleaning crew still works around it. Nobody is surprised.

You already knew this, of course. You knew it the moment you opened the door and looked in and closed it again without going further, which is the correct response, and also the universal one. Some doors should not be opened. The horror stall has always known this. It is the only one in the building that does.
