The 1st Annual
Crypt & Quill
Poetry Invitational

Where the dead find their voice — and really won't shut up about it

Reported live from Plot 7, Section C · Our correspondent was slightly decomposed

💀 ✦ 💀 ✦ 💀

It began, as all great literary movements do, with a noise complaint. Specifically, a rattling. Specifically, from underground. The graveyard committee of Whispering Pines Memorial Park had long tolerated the restless dead — a bump here, a phosphorescent glow there — but when six separate tombs began producing what can only be described as competitive creative output, something had to be done.

Thus was born the 1st Annual Crypt & Quill Poetry Invitational, the world's first (and let's be honest, only) literary competition in which 100% of contestants are skeletons, 0% of contestants have functioning lungs with which to perform spoken word, and the prize is a solid-gold femur trophy that the winner cannot feel, grip, or display in any meaningful way. Truly, art for art's sake.

Below, our esteemed panel of decomposed critics weighs in on each entry. Grab a shovel, pour yourself something murky, and enjoy the finest poetry to emerge from beneath the earth this century. Possibly any century.

Entry No. 001
The Midnight Itch
by Anonymous — though everyone suspects Gerald from Plot 12
I've rattled every rib tonight, this coffin's far too tight, The worms are dull conversationalists, the dirt lacks wit or bite. I tried to nap for centuries — what a tedious mistake — There's only so much "rest in peace" a skeleton can take. So I tap my femur thoughtfully and hum a hollow tune, And dream of dancing wildly in the chilly midnight moon. If someone hears me knocking, do be kind — just set me free! I've done my time in silence. Now these bones would like to flee.
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"Magnificent. The phrase 'worms are dull conversationalists' is the most accurate thing written in any century. We gave this poem top marks, then immediately questioned whether worms heard us and began composing a rebuttal." — Lord Bonsworth

Entry No. 002
Calcium Bliss
by Countess Vera Deadwick — "a romance in two stanzas"
I miss the pink bloom of your cheek's velvet glow, Back when we had pulses and muscles in tow. Now you are ivory, polished and spare, With a permanent grin and a lack of real hair. But why should I pine for a lip or a blush, In this graveyard silence, this hollowed-out hush? Come lean in your skull for a calcium bliss, And click-clack your jaw in a tooth-to-tooth kiss.
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"The most genuinely romantic poem submitted. 'Click-clack your jaw in a tooth-to-tooth kiss' made Madame Ossifère clutch what remains of her chest. Three of the judges wept. The fourth doesn't have tear ducts anymore, which he felt was relevant to mention." — Panel Consensus

🚨 Controversy Alert Several audience members protested that "a permanent grin and a lack of real hair" was a targeted attack on Judge Bonsworth personally. Bonsworth denied this, then grinned permanently, which didn't help his case.

Entry No. 003
The Restless Rattle
by B. Knees — "an insomniac's complaint"
I've rattled every rib tonight and clicked every toe, With nowhere to dance and nowhere to go. The worms are all snoring, the beetles are deep, But my knobby old kneecaps refuse any sleep. I'll pop up the lid with a creak and a clink, To find a nice puddle for a cool, muddy drink. I'm tired of the silence, the dark, and the pine, I've got a wild itch in my calcium spine!
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"A classic. Raw. Unfiltered. Desperately in need of hydration. 'A wild itch in my calcium spine' was immediately adopted as the official motto of the competition and is being embroidered on zero tote bags because none of the judges have hands that work properly." — Professor Xyloph

Entry No. 004 — SPECIAL CATEGORY: Literary Homage
Shall I Compare Thee to a Marble Tomb?
by The Shakespearian Skeleton — "submitted in iambic pentameter as God intended"
Shall I compare thee to a marble tomb? Thou art more polished and more pearly white. Rough winds do shake the lilies where they bloom, And burial lease hath all too short a night. Sometime too hot the sun of living glows, And often is his golden skin-tone shed; And every fair from fleshy fairness goes, By time or nature's hand, among the dead. But thy eternal structure shall not fade, Nor lose possession of that toothy grin; Nor shall Death brag thou wand'rest in his shade, When in eternal dust thou art tucked in. So long as rats can crawl or sockets see, So long lives this, and this gives life to thee.
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"Shakespeare's estate has been notified. They are deceased and therefore unavailable for comment. We award full marks for audacity, perfect marks for scansion, and a special bonus mark for 'tucked in,' which is somehow both tender and deeply unsettling. The line 'So long as rats can crawl or sockets see' will haunt us. It will haunt all of us." — Unanimous Panel

📜 Fun Fact This skeleton reportedly rehearsed for 200 years before submitting. Judges noted the polish. The skeleton noted it had literally nothing else to do.

Entry No. 005 — SPECIAL CATEGORY: Limerick
A Thirsty Old Skeleton, Mick
by An Irish Skeleton — "submitted from a pub in County Cork, somehow"
A thirsty old skeleton, Mick, Downed a glass of the Jameson quick. But it splashed through his ribs, Like the spills from small bibs, 'Tis a bone-dry and terrible trick!
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"Five lines. Perfect. An entire tragedy in limerick form. The physics of this poem were independently verified: whiskey does, in fact, pass straight through a ribcage. This was tested. We lost a very good Jameson. Professor Xyloph is still upset about it." — Lord Bonsworth, wiping nothing from his eye socket

🍺 Editor's Note Mick attempted to claim the physical trophy at the end of the evening. He then watched it fall through his fingers. He has requested the prize be converted to Jameson. The committee is considering it.

Entry No. 006 — SPECIAL CATEGORY: Rock & Roll
Ain't Nothin' But a Bone-Bag
by A Presley Poet — submitted with full hip gyration, which was audible
You ain't nothin' but a bone-bag, Rattlin' all the time! You ain't nothin' but a bone-bag, Rattlin' all the time! Well, you ain't never caught a beetle, And you ain't no friend of mine. When they said you was high-class, Well, that was just a lie. Yeah, they said you was high-class, Well, that was just a lie. 'Cause you ain't got no muscle, And you ain't got no eye!
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"We were not prepared. No one was prepared. The contestant entered the judging crypt doing what can only be described as a pelvis rotation with 30% fewer parts than the original choreography required. 'You ain't never caught a beetle' is a devastating diss in skeleton society, equivalent to calling someone uncultured. Minus two points for making Madame Ossifère spit out her grave water laughing. Plus twelve points for the same reason." — Unanimous Standing Ovation (none of them could stand, but the spirit was there)

Entry No. 007
I've Misplaced My Head
by Unknown — literally, the author could not be located
I'm feeling quite drafty above my top vert, And stumbling blindly is starting to hurt. I've patted the moss and I've felt every stone, But I can't find my roundest and brainiest bone. I bumped into marble and tripped on a root, Quite literally "losing my mind" to boot. Oh, where did I leave it? The crypt or the shed? It's so hard to think when you've misplaced your head!
🎙️ Judge's Commentary

"Our investigators searched the crypt, the shed, three adjacent plots, and what turned out to be a badger den. The head was eventually located behind the stone angel on Row 4. The angel refused to comment. This poem wins the special award for Most Autobiographical Entry, as the author was, for the entire judging period, living proof of its central thesis." — Security Report, filed simultaneously with judging notes

🧠 Medical Note Despite lacking a brain, the author demonstrated remarkable problem-solving ability by eventually reattaching their skull and immediately forgetting where they'd put it again. The judges felt this showed genuine artistic commitment to the theme.

🦴 Grand Champion · Crypt & Quill 2024 🦴
"The Midnight Itch"
by the skeleton from Plot 12 who we're 90% sure is Gerald

The panel unanimously agreed that "there's only so much 'rest in peace' a skeleton can take" is the defining sentence of the undead literary renaissance. The gold femur trophy has been presented. Gerald immediately dropped it into his own grave. It is now considered a permanent installation.

The 2nd Annual Crypt & Quill Poetry Invitational is scheduled for next Halloween, assuming everyone can find their various parts. Entry forms are available at the groundskeeper's hut. The groundskeeper does not know about the competition and has asked that everyone stop leaving parchment under his door at 3am.

If you are a skeleton reading this: we see you. We hear you rattling. Your suffering has been documented, your poetry has been judged, and your longing for a cool, muddy drink has been recognized by an international panel of your equally decomposed peers. Keep writing. Keep rattling. The worms may be sleeping, but literature never sleeps.

...unless it's been buried for 300 years, in which case, fair enough.
