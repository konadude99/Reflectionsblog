Raised Eyebrows  ·  Festival Edition  ·  Virginia City, Nevada  ·  Est. 1990s

At its peak in the mid-1870s, Virginia City, Nevada was the most important industrial city between Denver and San Francisco. The Comstock Lode — the first major silver deposit discovered in the United States — lay beneath its streets, and the men who ran its mines had accumulated fortunes that helped finance the transcontinental railroad, shaped San Francisco's banks, and generated enough political weight to push Nevada into statehood in 1864. Mark Twain got his pen name here, writing for the Territorial Enterprise. The city had 25,000 residents, more than 100 saloons, four banks, six churches, and Piper's Opera House. It was, for a moment, a genuine metropolis perched improbably on a Nevada hillside at 6,200 feet.

The mines ran out. The population went with them. By the turn of the century, Storey County had about 3,500 people where it once had 25,000. Today Virginia City's population is around 850. C Street — the main drag, where the saloons and shops and the Territorial Enterprise once operated in full Comstock-era splendor — is a National Historic Landmark District lined with Victorian buildings that have been carefully preserved, most of them now selling gold-rush souvenirs and offering tours.

And every October, C Street is closed to traffic so that outhouse racers can sprint down it in costume.

This is not a non sequitur. It makes a certain perfect sense, once you know the origin story.

The ProtestHow Banning Outhouses Created a Festival

In the early 1990s, Storey County installed a sewer system and asked residents to remove their outdoor toilets. This was a reasonable request — indoor plumbing being, generally, an improvement — but Virginia City was a town that had never done anything in quite the way other places did, and a significant number of residents were not prepared to simply comply. They had outhouses. They liked their outhouses. The county wanted those outhouses gone.

So they put their outhouses on wheels, dragged them to town, and raced them down C Street.

The exact details of who organized the first race and when it became an annual event vary somewhat in the telling, but the essential facts are consistent: county officials banned outdoor toilets, residents staged their protest by racing said toilets down the historic main street, someone realized this was considerably more entertaining than the alternative, and a tradition was born. Virginia City's tourism director Todd Tuttle has described it plainly: "People came with wagons or wheels, carts or whatever, and they realized, 'hey, this might be fun to create a race.'"

It was. The World Championship Outhouse Races have been running every October since — now in their late thirties as an annual event — and this is, as the official festival materials note with evident pride, the only outhouse race in the United States that began as a political protest.

The RaceHow It Works

The format is simple and has not required significant revision in thirty-plus years. Teams of three build a homemade outhouse — wheeled, human-powered, and required to have "some resemblance to something that you would have seen as an outhouse," a standard that has proven flexible enough to accommodate considerable artistic interpretation. One team member rides inside, seated on the throne. The other two push and pull the structure down C Street's historic boardwalk-lined corridor toward a finish line that is, fittingly, marked with a rope of toilet paper.

Teams race head-to-head, two outhouses at a time, in a bracket format. Saturday opens with the Parade of Outhouses — the entries processing down C Street in full costume and decoration before the qualifying heats begin. Sunday brings the semifinals and finals. The champion is named Fastest Outhouse in the West.

The costumes are, collectively, one of the better arguments for the race. Teams approach the decorating and costuming with genuine creative commitment. Past team names include The Pot Rod, The Classic Crapper, The Urinator, Who's on Turd, and El Baño — which won the 2025 championship on its crew's first race, in the team's 23rd year of competing under the name. "It feels good," said one of El Baño's pushers after the victory. "We're a little lighter on the stomach now that we've won the outhouse races." The team had been showing up for twenty-three years without a title. Their patience was evidently structural.

Official Race Requirements
World Championship Outhouse Races · Virginia City, Nevada · C Street
1
The outhouse must have some resemblance to something you would have seen as an outhouse. This requirement is interpreted generously.
2
Teams consist of three people: one rider who sits on the throne, and two who push and pull the structure down C Street. All must be in costume.
3
The outhouse must be human-powered. Engines, motors, and other mechanical propulsion are not permitted. The irony of this rule, given that the protest was against modernity, is not acknowledged in the official literature.
4
Races are head-to-head, two outhouses at a time. The finish line is marked with toilet paper. The champion is awarded the title of Fastest Outhouse in the West.
5
Spectators vote for Best Outhouse separately from the race result. This is considered a competitive honor of equal distinction to the speed title, though the literature is careful not to say so explicitly.

The SettingC Street Has Seen Worse

C Street was the commercial spine of the Comstock. The Territorial Enterprise, where a young Samuel Clemens first published under the name Mark Twain in 1863, stood here. So did saloons that served miners coming off 100-degree shifts three thousand feet underground, gambling houses, a brothel district, and the offices of the men who were extracting $36 million worth of gold and silver per year at the Comstock's peak. The street has survived fires, the collapse of the mining economy, and 150 years of slow population decline, emerging as a National Historic Landmark with its Victorian storefronts largely intact.

Against this backdrop, a team of three people in matching costumes sprinting an elaborately decorated outhouse on wheels past tourists on wooden boardwalks is, if anything, historically appropriate. Virginia City in its heyday had a flair for spectacle. Piper's Opera House hosted touring productions. The saloons had stages. The town understood that entertainment and commerce were not separate categories. The outhouse races fit neatly into that tradition — rowdy, public, slightly absurd, and free to attend.

Thousands line the boardwalks each October. The event generates real economic activity for a town whose population has settled at around 850. The Virginia City Tourism Commission organizes it. It is, as these things go, both genuinely funny and quietly essential.

El Baño, Champions

El Baño competed for 23 years before winning the 2025 championship. Their crew had changed — it was the crew's first race — but the outhouse had been showing up on C Street for over two decades.

Their victory statement: "We're a little lighter on the stomach now that we've won the outhouse races." This is either a racing metaphor, a digestive metaphor, or both, and the race does not require clarification.

Mark Twain Was Here

Samuel Clemens worked for the Territorial Enterprise on C Street from 1862 to 1864, first using the pen name "Mark Twain" in February 1863. He covered Virginia City's booming, brawling, improbable mining metropolis with a characteristic mixture of accuracy and embellishment.

He did not cover the outhouse races, which had not yet been invented. One assumes he would have enjoyed them.

The Haunted Outhouse

One competitor named Matt Tilley entered a Haunted Outhouse complete with a fictionalized 1800s backstory: "Julie Bulette made a wish on it for anyone who was building or touching the outhouse to have bad luck, because the guy that built the outhouse kept her up after a hard night's work."

Julia Bulette was a real Virginia City figure, a saloon owner and courtesan who was murdered in 1867 and became one of the town's most enduring legends. The outhouse races have, apparently, absorbed her into the canon.

World Championship Outhouse Races
Virginia City, Nevada  ·  October
The original political protest outhouse race, now in its mid-thirties as an annual event. C Street is cleared, teams of three sprint their rolling thrones in head-to-head heats, and the winner is named Fastest Outhouse in the West. Free to attend. Several thousand spectators. The most storied address in the western United States serves as the track. Mark Twain walked this street. Now El Baño does.
Fur Rendezvous Outhouse Races
Anchorage, Alaska  ·  February
Part of Fur Rondy, Anchorage's largest winter festival, which has been running since 1935 as a celebration timed to coincide with fur traders returning from the wilderness. The outhouses here race on skis down Fourth Avenue in February, pushed by four-person teams while a fifth member rides inside. Required equipment: a seat, a toilet paper holder, and a helmet for the rider. The race takes place on the same stretch of downtown where the Running of the Reindeer also happens, because Anchorage in February does not do things halfway.
Yale Bologna Festival Outhouse Races
Yale, Michigan  ·  July
Yale, Michigan — population under 2,000 — hosts the Yale Bologna Festival every July, drawing 20,000 people to celebrate the local C. Roy Processing plant's bologna, which has been made in Yale since 1924. The festival includes a Big Bologna Parade, a Bologna King and Queen pageant, a bologna ring toss, a Baby Bologna Contest, and outhouse races. The outhouse races are considered one of several equally valid festival highlights. This is correct, and also says something about Yale's sense of proportion.
Elk Falls Outhouse Tour
Elk Falls, Kansas  ·  November
Population: approximately 100. Elk Falls, Kansas, self-proclaimed Outhouse Capital of Kansas, holds its Outhouse Tour on the Friday and Saturday before Thanksgiving. This is not a race. It is a self-guided walking tour of the town's decorated outhouses, which have been painted with murals, fitted with sculptures, and otherwise transformed into works of art. For one dollar, visitors receive a commemorative Outhouse Button, a tour map, and a ballot to vote for their favorite privy. The winning outhouse receives recognition. This is the contemplative, non-competitive end of the outhouse-events spectrum, and it has its own dignity.

The Larger PictureWhy Anyone Races an Outhouse

The question, fairly asked, is why this particular form of festival caught on. Outhouse races require building a sizable wooden structure, mounting it on wheels, costuming a team of three, transporting the whole apparatus to a historic Nevada mining town in October, and then sprinting it down a street while spectators on 150-year-old boardwalks cheer. The payoff is a toilet paper finish line and the title of Fastest Outhouse in the West.

And yet it has been happening every year for over thirty years, and in Alaska it has been happening even longer, and in Michigan it happens as a side event to a bologna festival, and in Kansas a town of a hundred people has built an entire tourism identity around standing still and looking at decorated ones. Something about the outhouse, as a cultural object, apparently invites this treatment.

Part of it is the toilet humor, which requires no explanation and provides an accessible entry point for any age. Part of it is the DIY creativity the format demands — building a racing outhouse is a genuine craft project, and the results, as the team names suggest, reward creative investment. But there is also something more specific about Virginia City: a town that was once the most important mining city in the American West, that was famous for its willingness to do things at full volume and without apology, found out that the county wanted to take its outhouses, and decided the appropriate response was to race them.

This is not a decline. This is a town that has always known how to throw a scene. The same instinct that built Piper's Opera House at 6,200 feet in the Nevada desert and opened more than a hundred saloons in a single mining camp is the instinct that puts a rolling throne on wheels and sprints it down C Street with the entire history of the American West watching from the boardwalks.

The ore ran out in 1878. The outhouses, it turns out, still run.
