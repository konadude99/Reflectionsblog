Health Policy Report · May 2026

The State of American Healthcare

A system under pressure — from Capitol Hill cuts to vanishing maternity wards, the coverage landscape is shifting beneath millions of Americans' feet.

Updated with 2026 Data  |  Sources: KFF · CBO · RAND · Commonwealth Fund · AHA · AAMC

American healthcare in May 2026 is not merely unequal — it is actively contracting. The fragile coverage gains of the pandemic era are unraveling: enhanced ACA subsidies have expired, Medicaid faces its largest cuts in history, rural maternity wards are closing at record pace, and a budget law signed on Independence Day 2025 promises to remove millions more from the insured rolls within a decade. The system still produces world-class medicine. The question is who can reach it.

Section 1

Health Insurance: Gains Reversed, New Pressures Mounting

For most of the 2020s, the headline story of American health insurance was progress. Medicaid expansion spread to 41 states (including D.C.). Pandemic-era policies swelled rolls to historic highs. ACA marketplace enrollment hit a record 24.3 million in 2025. But 2026 marks a decisive pivot — and not in the right direction.

The subsidy cliff has returned. The enhanced premium tax credits first enacted under the American Rescue Plan Act (2021) and extended by the Inflation Reduction Act (2022) expired at the end of 2025. Congress did not renew them. The result: average out-of-pocket ACA marketplace premiums jumped from $113/month in 2025 to $178/month in 2026 — a 57% increase in what enrollees pay directly. For the roughly 8 million who were auto-renewed without actively re-selecting a plan, the shock is landing gradually, and a significant share are expected to drop coverage when the grace period ends.

For middle-income households earning just above 400% of the federal poverty level — roughly $58,000 for an individual — the "subsidy cliff" has returned with particular brutality. A couple in their late 50s earning $85,000 in West Virginia, for instance, could see their lowest-cost plan jump from affordable to more than half their annual income.

Enrollment data confirms the pullback. 23.1 million people enrolled in marketplace plans for 2026 — down 4.9% from the prior year's record. CMS attributed over 1.5 million of the drop to fraud prevention efforts that had been paused since COVID; the rest reflects genuine unaffordability-driven exits. The share of enrollees receiving any subsidy fell from 92% in 2025 to 87% in 2026.

Meanwhile, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law July 4, 2025, has begun reshaping Medicaid — the program covering roughly 79 million low-income Americans. Its provisions include work requirements (80 hours/month for non-disabled adults, beginning December 2026), six-month rather than annual eligibility redeterminations (beginning 2027), new co-pays up to $35 per service for expansion enrollees, and limits on the provider taxes states use to draw down federal matching funds. The Congressional Budget Office estimates these changes will cause 10 million Americans to lose Medicaid coverage by 2034, with 2 million losing coverage in 2026 alone.

Texas continues to lead the nation in uninsured residents, with 16.7% of its population lacking coverage — and 21.6% of working-age adults. The state accounts for 42% of the national "coverage gap": the approximately 1.4 million Americans in non-expansion states who earn too much for traditional Medicaid but too little for marketplace subsidies. Oklahoma (11.5%), Georgia (12%), and Florida (10.9%) face similar pressures.

Section 2

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act: A Structural Inflection Point

No single piece of legislation has reshaped American healthcare policy since the ACA quite like the OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025. It represents the largest federal rollback of Medicaid in the program's history, cutting $911 billion in federal Medicaid spending over ten years according to CBO estimates.

RAND Health's analysis estimates that state Medicaid budgets will collectively lose $665 billion over the 2025–2034 period — with general fund spending reductions of $86 billion on top of that. The states facing the steepest relative cuts include Arizona, Iowa, and Nevada (projected reductions exceeding 15% of Medicaid budgets), largely because of caps on the provider tax financing mechanisms those states relied upon most heavily.

"The OBBB cuts over $1 trillion from health programs — the largest rollback of federal support for healthcare in American history."
— Center for Medicare Advocacy, July 2025

As partial mitigation, the law created a $50 billion Rural Health Transformation Program ($10 billion per year, 2026–2030). But KFF analysis shows this offsets only a fraction of the damage: the rural health fund front-loads money before most cuts take effect, nearly two-thirds of the ten-year Medicaid reductions occur after fiscal year 2030, and CMS stipulated no more than 15% of funds may go directly to hospitals or patient care. KFF estimates rural Medicaid spending will still fall by $137 billion over ten years, with rural hospitals' Medicaid revenue dropping as much as 9.6% on average and uncompensated care rising 35.4%.

Medicare is also affected, though less directly. The OBBBA increases the federal deficit sufficiently to trigger automatic PAYGO cuts — the CBO estimates a $45 billion Medicare reduction in 2026, growing to $75 billion annually by 2034. It also narrows Medicare eligibility for lawfully present non-citizen immigrants and exempts orphan drugs from the drug-price negotiation authority granted by the Inflation Reduction Act, limiting one of Medicare's few cost-control tools.

Section 3

Rural America: A Healthcare System in Retreat

The rural healthcare crisis, long simmering, is now visibly accelerating. As of mid-2026, 6,100 hospitals operate in the United States — down from 6,146 just five years earlier. The shrinkage is concentrated in rural areas. According to Public Citizen's analysis, 446 hospitals are at heightened risk from Medicaid cuts, defined by a Medicaid payer mix over 20% combined with negative profit margins. The Center for Healthcare Quality and Payment Reform (CHQPR) finds that 734 rural hospitals — roughly one in three — are currently operating in the red, with 323 facing imminent closure risk within two to three years.

The maternity care crisis is the sharpest visible symptom. Since 2020, 116 rural labor and delivery units have closed. In 2025 alone, 27 rural hospitals closed or announced closures of their L&D services — the second-highest single-year total on record. Only 41% of rural hospitals now provide maternity services at all. In roughly a third of all U.S. counties, there is not a single obstetric provider or birthing facility. Rural pregnant women must travel an average of 30 minutes to reach a hospital with L&D services; in more remote communities, the drive exceeds 50 minutes.

Real-world consequences are appearing rapidly. In November 2025, St. Mary's Sacred Heart in rural Georgia cited "physician recruitment challenges, increasing outmigration for labor and delivery services, and recent Congressional cuts to Medicaid" as reasons for ending maternal health services. In January 2026, Greene County General Hospital in Indiana ended obstetrics. Trinity Health System, which operates 92 hospitals, announced a 10.5% reduction in revenue cycle staff, noting that "recent and future government funding policy changes" could lower its annual revenue by $1.5 billion.

Section 4

Physician Workforce: Maldistribution Over Shortage

The U.S. does not simply have too few doctors — it has the wrong doctors in the wrong places. The 2026 Main Residency Match was the largest in history, with 41,482 positions filled. Primary care offered more than 20,700 positions. Yet as the AAMC's workforce models project, the nation faces a physician shortfall of 13,500 to 86,000 by 2036, concentrated in primary care (20,200–40,400 physicians short) and surgical specialties.

The structural driver behind all specialty shortages is the same: physicians choose locations based on income, quality of life, and professional opportunity — not community need. Urban areas with academic medical centers continue to attract the bulk of specialists. Rural and low-income communities are left competing for a shrinking residual supply, often at a significant wage disadvantage.

There are now 8,160 designated primary care Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs) in the United States. To eliminate those designations, HRSA estimates nearly 17,000 additional practitioners would be needed. The U.S. is projected to lack 3.2 million total healthcare workers by 2026, according to some workforce models — a figure that encompasses nurses, allied health, mental health workers, and support staff as much as physicians.

Section 6

Medicare, Medicare Advantage, and the Aging Country

Medicare now covers more than 66 million Americans. Medicare Advantage (MA) — the privately administered alternative to traditional Medicare — has grown to cover a majority of Medicare beneficiaries, driven by extra benefits, cap on out-of-pocket costs, and aggressive marketing. But MA programs have faced sustained scrutiny over prior authorization denials, aggressive diagnostic coding that inflates payments, and narrow networks that restrict access.

The OBBBA accelerates Medicare's fiscal pressures through PAYGO mechanisms. If automatic sequestration cuts are triggered and not waived by Congress, Medicare faces reductions of $45 billion in 2026 rising to $75 billion annually by 2034. The Medicare Hospital Insurance Trust Fund's insolvency timeline has also been moved forward by the deficit expansion the law entails.

The deeper structural challenge remains unchanged: America is aging rapidly. By 2034, the number of Americans over 65 will be 77 million, up from 58 million in 2022. Chronic disease burden, dementia prevalence, and long-term care needs are all rising — against a backdrop of severe geriatrician shortages, nursing home staffing crises, and home health worker scarcity. The math is unforgiving, and no current federal policy addresses it at scale.

Medicare Drug Pricing: A Partial Victory, Partially Reversed

The Inflation Reduction Act's landmark drug-price negotiation authority took effect in 2026, with the first negotiated prices now covering 10 selected Medicare drugs — the first time Medicare has ever directly negotiated drug prices in its 60-year history. Early negotiations have produced meaningful savings on some high-cost medications for diabetes and cardiovascular disease. But the OBBBA's carve-out of orphan drugs limits the program's reach, and drug companies have filed multiple legal challenges to the negotiation framework that remain unresolved in the courts.

Section 7

The Cost of American Healthcare: World-Leading Spending, Middling Outcomes

The United States spends 17.2% of GDP on healthcare — by far the highest among wealthy nations, roughly twice the OECD average. Yet on most population health metrics, U.S. outcomes are mediocre relative to peer nations: higher maternal mortality, lower life expectancy, worse chronic disease control, and worse infant mortality than comparably wealthy countries.

The cost structure of American healthcare is driven by administrative complexity (estimated at 25–35% of total spending), provider price variation, pharmaceutical pricing, and the fee-for-service model's incentive toward volume rather than value. Telehealth remains one of the system's genuine bright spots — pandemic-era regulatory flexibilities have largely been preserved, expanding access particularly in behavioral health, chronic disease management, and rural primary care.

Section 8

The Bottom Line: May 2026

American healthcare in the spring of 2026 sits at an inflection point more severe than any since the ACA's passage in 2010 — but this time, the movement is in the opposite direction.

The coverage gains of the past five years are being systematically unwound: enhanced subsidies have expired, Medicaid faces historic cuts, rural hospitals are closing at an accelerating pace, and a law with trillion-dollar consequences for low-income Americans was signed into law before most of its effects have been felt. Meanwhile, the physician workforce maldistribution, specialty bottlenecks, and maternity care deserts continue to deepen.

The American healthcare paradox — world-class medicine alongside vast gaps in access — has not been resolved. It has widened. And the structural forces driving that widening are, for the most part, now law.

That is the state of American healthcare in May 2026.
