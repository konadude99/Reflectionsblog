Companion Piece · Part Two of Two

The Great American
Drive-In Road Trip

A deliberately structured journey across 4,200 miles of blacktop, nostalgia, and surprisingly strict parking lot rules

Durango, CO → Cape Cod, MA

◆ The Route — Southwest to Northeast ◆

📎 This is the companion piece to Honk If You Love Cinema — a history and love letter to American drive-in theaters. That article explains why drive-ins exist. This one tells you how to go see the survivors yourself.

Here is the thing about planning a drive-in road trip: you cannot plan it like a normal road trip. You cannot draw a line on a map, calculate mileage, and book hotels. Drive-ins run on dusk, not distance. They open when the sky darkens and close when the last credits roll. Many are only alive Thursday through Sunday. Rain can cancel an entire evening. The whole enterprise must be organized around one thing: the night.

What follows is a route across eleven surviving drive-ins — from the high desert of New Mexico to the salt air of Cape Cod — structured to trace something larger than just the theaters themselves. You're watching the entire evolution of an American institution in real time, state by state, parking lot by parking lot.

"You're not just visiting theaters. You're watching an idea evolve across geography, economics, and time." — From the trip notes

Start in Durango. Head southwest into New Mexico. Arc through Texas, cut northeast across Route 66, grind through the Midwest core, and finally arrive in Pennsylvania and Maryland — where the most obsessive, most peculiar, most genuinely unforgettable drive-in experiences in America quietly await. Cape Cod is there if you still have gas in the tank and wonder in your heart.

It is approximately 4,200 miles. Plan for 10 to 14 nights. Bring bug spray.

I
Leg One · Colorado & New Mexico
The High Desert Warm-Up
Authentic. Stripped-down. Old-school.

You start here not because New Mexico has the most impressive drive-ins — it doesn't — but because it has the most honest ones. No food trucks, no curated playlists, no Instagram lighting. Just a big screen, a field of gravel, the dark, and the sky. This is the baseline. This is what a drive-in actually was for most of its life.

Stop 1
Sky Vu Drive-In
📍 Farmington, New Mexico
Classic Old-School

No frills. Local crowd. The parking lot smells like dust and the concession stand sells hot dogs in the way that hot dogs were always meant to be sold: under fluorescent lights, by someone who has been doing this for twenty years.

Why it matters: This is your calibration stop. Everything you see after this will be measured against it. You need to know what unadorned actually looks like before you encounter the reinventors.

Stop 2
Comanche Drive-In
📍 Buena Vista, Colorado
Classic

Set against the Collegiate Peaks. On a clear night, you'll watch a movie with 14,000-foot mountains as your backdrop, which is either awe-inspiring or deeply distracting depending on your temperament. The air at altitude has a particular clarity that makes the screen glow differently.

Why it matters: Geography transforms the drive-in experience. The same format, the same screen, the same popcorn — and yet completely different because of where you are. The first of many lessons this trip will teach.

Stop 3
Holiday Twin Drive-In
📍 Fort Collins, Colorado
Classic

Twin screens. Stronger community vibe. A more polished operation than Farmington, but not yet the reinvented model coming in Texas. Think of it as the middle ground — a drive-in that has evolved slightly without abandoning its original character.

Why it matters: You'll notice the difference between a drive-in that has adapted and one that hasn't, and realize neither answer is wrong. The trip is already teaching you nuance.

II
Leg Two · Texas
The Reinvention Corridor
Modernized. Social. Hospitality-first.

Texas figured something out that most drive-ins didn't: the movie is not the product. The night out is the product. Film is just the anchor around which a social experience is organized. Once you accept this, everything else follows — food trucks, craft beer, lawn chairs, lawn games. The drive-in becomes something closer to an outdoor festival that happens to be showing a Spielberg film.

Stop 4
Coyote Drive-In
📍 Fort Worth, Texas
Modern Social-First

Food trucks. A proper bar. Lawn seating. You can watch from your car, or you can spread a blanket on the grass and eat tacos while a film plays above you. The staff look like they work at a good restaurant, not a 1957 snack bar. The movie is almost beside the point — but it's a remarkably pleasant beside-the-point.

Why it matters: This is what the future of the drive-in almost certainly looks like. If the model survives at scale, it will be because someone kept doing exactly this. It works. Frustratingly, magnificently, it works.

Stop 5
Stars & Stripes Drive-In
📍 Lubbock or New Braunfels, Texas
Traditional Upgraded

Retro branding, but operationally sharp. Family-focused. Clean. Well-run. A drive-in that has invested in the experience without abandoning what made drive-ins worth visiting in the first place. A useful contrast with Coyote — same state, different philosophy, both alive.

Why it matters: Two Texas drive-ins, two viable survival strategies. One reinvented. One refined. Neither nostalgic in a sad way.

III
Leg Three · Missouri
The Route 66 Nostalgia Spine
Americana. Preservation. Memory.

Route 66 has always been less a road than a myth you can drive on. The 66 Drive-In in Carthage fits directly into that tradition — a place where the past is not merely preserved but actively inhabited. This leg is about context as much as cinema. Stop at the roadside diners. Read the historical markers. Let the Americana accumulate.

Stop 6
66 Drive-In
📍 Carthage, Missouri
Historic Americana

It feels frozen in time in exactly the way that something must intend to feel frozen in time — because the alternative was being bulldozed. The Route 66 branding is not cynical; it's honest. This place knows what it is and wears it without embarrassment.

Why it matters: Some drive-ins survive by modernizing. This one survives by refusing to. Understanding both strategies is the whole point of the trip.

IV
Leg Four · Illinois & Wisconsin
The Midwest Core
Where the model still simply works.

If you want to understand why drive-ins in the Midwest outlasted drive-ins on the coasts, you need only look at a real estate map. Land in Gibson City, Illinois is not what land in Los Angeles is. The fundamental economics that killed drive-ins everywhere else — the land-is-worth-more-than-the-business calculation — simply didn't apply here. What you find instead are drive-ins that have been running for decades because the numbers actually work.

Stop 7
Harvest Moon Twin Drive-In
📍 Gibson City, Illinois
Community

Families. Regulars. People who have been coming here for twenty, thirty, forty years. Kids who are now bringing their own kids. This is the drive-in as community infrastructure — not a novelty, not a destination, just the thing you do on a summer Friday night in Gibson City, Illinois, because that's what you've always done.

Why it matters: The economic sweet spot of the surviving drive-in: low land cost, loyal audience, low overhead, genuine local identity. This is the model that doesn't require reinvention to survive.

Stop 8 ★ Optional
Skyline Drive-In
📍 Baraboo, Wisconsin
Rustic

More rustic, further north, slightly more unpredictable. Worth adding if your schedule allows. Baraboo is also home to the Ringling Brothers Circus museum, which gives the detour a pleasing "things that are genuinely American and slightly improbable" coherence.

Optional, but adds welcome variation before the intensity of the Pennsylvania leg.

V
Leg Five · Pennsylvania
The Purist Pilgrimages
Obsession. Cinephile energy. Living history.

Pennsylvania is where the drive-in trip becomes a pilgrimage. These two stops are not merely interesting — they are genuinely, historically significant. One operates as a shrine to analog cinema. The other has been open since 1934. Between them, they represent something the other stops on this route do not: the drive-in not as an adapted business, but as an act of will.

Stop 9
Mahoning Drive-In
📍 Lehighton, Pennsylvania
35mm Only Cult Destination

The Mahoning shows only 35mm film prints. Not as an affectation or a marketing hook, but because the people who run it believe digital projection is a form of moral compromise. Themed weekends draw cinephiles from across the country — horror marathons, blaxploitation double bills, spaghetti westerns. The crowds are passionate in the way that only people who have driven four hours for a specific film print can be passionate.

Why it matters: You're sitting in a field watching film grain flicker on a screen while people around you weep quietly about the fact that this still exists. It is, genuinely, a little moving.

Stop 10
Shankweiler's Drive-In
📍 Orefield, Pennsylvania
Est. 1934 Oldest Operating

Open since 1934. Claims to be the oldest operating drive-in in America. It has been running continuously for over ninety years, which means it has survived: World War II, television, the VCR, the DVD, streaming, a pandemic, and the digital projection crisis. Whatever they are doing, it is clearly correct.

Why it matters: You are sitting inside living history. Richard Hollingshead patented the drive-in in 1933. Shankweiler's opened the following year. Do the math.

VI
Leg Six · Maryland
The Rules & Ritual Experience
Structured. Intense. Almost ceremonial.

Bengies Drive-In in Baltimore County is unlike any other venue on this list. It operates with a level of procedural discipline that is either maddening or deeply comforting, depending on your relationship with authority. The rules are extensive. The enforcement is consistent. The triple features are long. You will arrive at a specific time. You will park in a specific way. You will watch all three films.

Stop 11
Bengies Drive-In
📍 Baltimore County, Maryland
Triple Features Legendary

The rules are posted. The rules are enforced. There will be a pre-show announcement reminding you of the rules. You will not honk your horn casually. You will tune to the correct frequency. You will not leave early. And somehow — inexplicably, entirely against expectations — it all works. The ritual creates a strange collective focus that most outdoor events lack entirely.

Why it's unforgettable: Bengies turns moviegoing into a controlled ceremony. It is polarizing and magnificent and completely unlike anywhere else. Budget your entire evening. You will be there for a while.

VII
Leg Seven · Massachusetts
The Optional Coastal Finish
Atmospheric. Chaotic. Fittingly unpredictable.
Stop 12
Wellfleet Drive-In
📍 Cape Cod, Massachusetts
Hybrid Venue Flea Market + Cinema

By day, a flea market. By night, a drive-in theater. The parking lot smells of salt air and aged vinyl records. Raccoons patrol the perimeter. The movies start when the sun drops into the Atlantic. It is a fittingly chaotic, fittingly American, fittingly wonderful way to end a trip that began in the high desert of New Mexico approximately 4,200 miles and a dozen epiphanies ago.

Why it's the right ending: Every drive-in on this route had a clear identity, a clear model, a clear reason for surviving. Wellfleet is the exception that proves the rule — surviving through sheer atmospheric weirdness. You need that to feel complete.

What You're Actually Watching

By the time you reach Cape Cod, you'll have observed three distinct survival models — the whole evolutionary history of the drive-in, laid out across American geography:

Model What It Is Where You Saw It Why It Survives
The Original Simple, local, functional. No reinvention. Farmington NM, Gibson City IL Low land cost + loyal community = working economics.
The Adapted Social, experience-driven. Film as anchor, not product. Fort Worth TX, Cape Cod MA Hospitality model replaces theater model. Profitable.
The Preserved Historical and niche. Survival through specificity. Lehighton PA, Orefield PA Cult audience travels for what can't be found elsewhere.
The Ceremonial Rules, ritual, total commitment. Love it or leave it. Baltimore County MD Strong identity creates fierce loyalty and repeat attendance.

How to Actually Execute This Trip

Think in Nights, Not Miles

Drive-ins start at dusk — roughly 8:30 to 10 PM depending on the season and latitude. Your day's driving ends early enough to arrive, orient yourself, and get settled before showtime. This is not a trip where you drive eight hours and then watch a movie. Plan two to three hours of driving on screening days, maximum.

Check Schedules Religiously

Most surviving drive-ins operate Thursday through Sunday only. Programming changes weekly. Some have specific events — horror marathons at Mahoning, for instance — that require booking far in advance. Check websites before finalizing each leg. Build your nights around confirmed showtimes, not the other way around.

Weather Is a First-Class Risk

Rain can cancel a screening entirely. Fog degrades the image. In the South and Midwest, summer thunderstorms materialize from clear skies in forty minutes. Build at least two or three slack nights into your itinerary — extra days you can absorb without cascading delays. Consider them insurance, not padding.

⚙ Bengies Rule: Plan Your Entire Evening Around Stop 11

  • → Triple features run until approximately 2 AM. This is not an exaggeration.
  • → Arrive at the stated time. The rules are posted and the staff take them seriously.
  • → Do not honk your horn recreationally. You will regret this.
  • → The concession stand is the primary revenue source. Act accordingly.
  • → Book a nearby hotel in advance. You will not want to drive far at 2 AM.

Bring the Right Gear

📻

Portable Radio

Modern drive-ins broadcast audio via FM. A portable radio lets you sit outside the car without losing sound.

🧣

Blankets & Layers

Even in summer, evenings cool down. Especially true in Colorado and the high desert. Non-negotiable.

🪑

Camp Chairs

Essential for hybrid venues like Coyote in Fort Worth where lawn seating is part of the experience.

🦟

Bug Spray

The Midwest and the South. Enough said. Bring the industrial grade. The mosquitoes are not joking.

🍿

Cash

Many drive-ins are cash-only at the concession stand. The concession stand is how they stay alive. Use it.

📖

Patience

Things start late. Things get rained out. Programming changes. This is not a failure mode. This is the experience.

Embrace the Variability

Some stops on this route will feel polished. Others will feel like they are held together by habit and stubbornness and a deeply felt conviction that someone has to keep doing this. Both are correct. The contrast between a Coyote Drive-In night — food trucks, string lights, craft beer — and a Mahoning Drive-In night — 35mm grain, folding chairs, a crowd of people who drove three hours for a specific horror film — is not a design flaw in the itinerary. It is the itinerary.

✦ ✦ ✦

A Final Observation

A drive-in road trip is, on its surface, a trip to watch movies in parking lots. Underneath that, it is something considerably more interesting: a tour of how a business model adapts — or refuses to — under decades of compounding pressure.

You'll visit places that survived by modernizing without shame, places that survived by refusing to change at all, and places that survived by becoming so specific, so niche, so themselves, that no competitor could replicate them. You'll find communities where the drive-in is still just the thing you do on Friday night, and communities where the drive-in is a destination that people travel across the country to reach.

Every one of them is right. Every one of them found a different answer to the same question.

You leave Durango wondering if drive-ins are nostalgia or something still genuinely alive. You arrive at Cape Cod knowing the answer: both, depending on which parking lot you're sitting in.

Start planning around the May–September window. Check operating schedules before you leave. The Mahoning books up for special events. Bengies does not care if you arrive late — you simply will not be admitted. And please, for the love of everything, bring the bug spray.
