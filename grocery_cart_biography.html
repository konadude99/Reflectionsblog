The Grocery Cart
with One Bad Wheel:
A Biography

It came into your life without warning. It had a plan. This is the fully documented story of the worst object in the known universe, and what it wants from you.

Filed under Raised Eyebrows  ·  Investigated Under Fluorescent Lighting  ·  Peer-reviewed by everyone in Aisle 7 who watched

Cart with Cherub and Despairing Shopper, oil on canvas, artist unknown, provenance disputed, currently on loan from the permanent collection of Human Suffering.  |  Shutterstock

It was waiting for you. You should know that. Among the forty or fifty carts jammed in the corral near the entrance, it was waiting — patient, practiced, utterly still — for a shopper who looked like they were already running slightly late and had a lot to get done. It looked fine. They always look fine. The wheel that is going to destroy your next forty-five minutes was not visibly wobbling. The cart was not making the sound yet. The sound only begins once you have committed — once you are past the entrance, past the point of easy return, with your handbag in the basket and your list half-consulted and the Saturday-morning grocery crowd pressing in behind you. That is when it starts. That is when the cart, having selected you, reveals its nature.

The grocery cart with one bad wheel is not a manufacturing defect. It is a philosophical position. It has decided, at some point in its long institutional life, that forward is a suggestion, that turning left is a negotiation, and that the relationship between the person gripping its handle and the direction of travel is one it intends to complicate at every opportunity. It does not merely malfunction. It dissents.

Chapter I · Early Life Origins and Formation of Character

A Brief Technical Note

The grocery cart wheel is a swivel caster, designed to rotate freely on a vertical axis and roll freely on a horizontal one. When functioning, it is an elegant piece of engineering.

When not functioning, it is a wheel-shaped piece of evil that has somehow retained just enough structural integrity to avoid being retired, while losing all of the functionality that would have made it worth keeping.

This is its ideal state. It has found it. It is staying there.

Supermarket carts, as a category, have a hard life. They are pushed by strangers, banged into curbs, left in the rain, occasionally returned from three blocks away by a conscientious stranger and more often not returned at all. They are crashed into each other at speed by people who genuinely believe the crash was unavoidable. They carry, over the course of a week, the combined weight of forty families’ groceries, the occasional toddler, and, in one documented case per store, a medium-sized dog.

Under these conditions, the wheels — each a small swivel caster made of plastic and optimism — begin to acquire what engineers call “debris fouling” and what regular people call “something got stuck in there and everything got worse.” Hair, string, the corner of a plastic bag, a rogue twist tie — any of these, wound around the axle, can reduce a wheel from freely swiveling to stubbornly refusing, from gliding to grinding, from an instrument of conveyance to an instrument of psychological torment.

What is not adequately explained by engineering is why the cart is never, in this condition, taken out of service. The answer, one suspects, involves a spreadsheet and someone who has never personally had to navigate the cart in question to the back of a Saturday Costco.

Chapter II · The Revelation The Sound, and What It Means

The sound begins somewhere between the produce section and the bread aisle. It is a squeak with ambitions — not content to be a small, apologetic squeak, but building, with each revolution of the afflicted wheel, toward something that could be charitably described as a rhythmic industrial complaint and uncharitably described as what a very small, very unhappy creature sounds like when it is being stepped on repeatedly in a quiet room.

The sound builds with each revolution toward something that could be charitably described as a rhythmic industrial complaint and uncharitably described as what a very small, very unhappy creature sounds like when it is being stepped on in a quiet room.

The squeak has a frequency. The frequency is precisely calibrated to carry. It bounces off the tile floors and the high ceilings and the refrigerated cases and arrives, amplified and clarified, at the ears of every other person in the store. Those people look up. They look at you. Their faces carry a unified expression that manages to combine sympathy (they have been there), amusement (it is not them, today), and the faint, unmistakable suggestion that you are making this noise on purpose, or at the very minimum that you could stop it if you really wanted to and are choosing not to.

You cannot stop it. You have already tried going slower. Slower makes it worse. You have tried going faster, which briefly masked the squeak behind the general noise of forward momentum before the cart responded to your acceleration by attempting to turn ninety degrees to the left without warning, requiring a full-body correction that drew more attention than the squeak had. You have tried lifting the front end slightly, which helped for four feet and then made the handle impossible to steer. You are committed now. You have milk in the cart. You are going to finish this shop.

Chapter III · Personality A Taxonomy of Its Methods

The bad-wheel cart is not monolithic. Years of field research — conducted by ordinary people under fluorescent lights, against their will, without compensation — have established that it operates through a repertoire of distinct techniques, each deployed situationally and with a timing that strongly suggests premeditation.

The Drift

The cart moves forward but also, mysteriously, sideways. You are aiming for the pasta. You are arriving at the wine. You have not changed your grip. The cart has simply decided that your destination and its destination are different, and that its destination takes priority. You correct. It drifts back. You correct again. This continues until you reach the pasta, exhausted, having covered a path that, if plotted on a map, would resemble a drunk trying to describe a straight line.

Threat Level: Quietly Relentless

The Sudden Stop

You are pushing. The cart is moving. The cart is not moving. You are still pushing. Nothing in the laws of physics adequately prepares you for the experience of applying forward force to an object that has simply decided, midway through an aisle, to stop. Your body continues forward. The cart does not. Groceries shift. You make a noise.

Threat Level: Structurally Surprising

The Hard Left

Executed, always, in a narrow aisle with someone coming the other way. The cart does not drift left; it commits left, at an angle that suggests it has spotted something interesting on that side and is going there regardless. The correction required is essentially a wrestling maneuver. The person coming the other way watches this with the careful neutral expression of someone who would like to offer help but senses that help has not been requested and might not go well.

Threat Level: Publicly Witnessed

The Shimmy

At certain speeds, the bad wheel does not pull or stop or squeak. It vibrates. The vibration travels up the handle and into your hands and up your arms and arrives, muffled but persistent, somewhere around your shoulder blades. After twenty minutes it is less a vibration than a mood. You are not angry. You are simply subtly, continuously, physically disagreed with.

Threat Level: Existentially Wearing

The Selective Cooperation

The most psychologically sophisticated technique. For one full aisle — usually an aisle you don’t need anything in — the cart rolls perfectly. Smoothly, quietly, straight. You think: perhaps I misjudged it. Perhaps it has resolved its issues. You turn into the next aisle. The squeak resumes, louder than before, as if refreshed.

Threat Level: Hope, Then Renewed Despair

The Spectacle

The cart is loudest in silence. In the freezer aisle, where the ambient noise is low, the compressors are rhythmic, and six other shoppers are reading labels in quiet concentration, the bad wheel finds its full voice. There is no talking over it. There is no pretending it is not you. You are the event.

Chapter IV · The Question of Intent Is It Doing This on Purpose?

The rational answer is no. The grocery cart is a steel-and-plastic object with no interior life, no memory, no capacity for planning or malice, and no awareness of you whatsoever. It is not doing this on purpose because it cannot do anything on purpose. It is a cart.

The rational answer is not, however, something you are capable of accessing by minute twelve.

By minute twelve, you are not dealing with a piece of retail infrastructure. You are dealing with a personality. A difficult personality, which has made choices, and which is not open to discussing those choices.

By minute twelve, you are not dealing with a piece of retail infrastructure. You are dealing with a personality. A difficult personality, which has made choices, and which is not open to discussing those choices. The cart has the specific energy of a family member who knows exactly what they are doing and has simply decided that your preferences are interesting to them as data but not binding as instructions. You push right; it goes left. You urge it forward; it pauses to consider. You slow down; it takes this as permission to audition for the role of factory machinery.

There is also the question of the cherub. Anyone who has stood in a supermarket aisle, white-knuckling a cart that is heading for the display of artfully stacked soup cans with what can only be described as purpose, will have had the fleeting, hallucinatory sense that something is laughing. Something small, somewhere above the eyeline, deeply entertained. The great painters understood this. They knew that human suffering at the hands of inanimate objects is not merely a modern inconvenience but a cosmic theme, and they rendered it accordingly, in oil, on very large canvases, with cherubs.

Chapter V · The Psychology of the Shopper Stages of Grief, Condensed

Average Minutes Before Abandoning All Dignity 7 based on field observation · margin of error: depends on the day

The shopper who has drawn the bad-wheel cart passes through recognizable stages. Denial comes first: perhaps the squeak will stop. Perhaps it was temporary. Perhaps the wheel merely needed to warm up, as one would warm up before exercise, and is now prepared to contribute constructively to the journey. BargainingAnger arrives somewhere in the dairy section, is managed, mostly, and resurfaces in the checkout queue when the cart, freed from your grip for thirty seconds while you get out your wallet, slowly and quietly rolls into the person behind you. Acceptance comes last, and it has a very specific face: the look of someone who has decided to simply get the milk and the eggs and the bread and go home and not speak of this to anyone, and who nearly makes it, and then tells everyone immediately upon arrival.

Chapter VI · The Return And Why You’ll Get It Again

You return the cart. You collect your groceries from the car. You go home. A week passes, or two, or three, and you go back to the store, and you walk to the cart corral, and you pull a cart, and for three aisles it is perfect, and then the squeak begins.

Is it the same cart? Impossible to say. The bad-wheel cart has no distinguishing features, no name, no serial number visible to the civilian eye. It could be a different cart entirely, newly afflicted, a new addition to the ranks. Or it could be the same one. The one that knew you were coming. The one that was waiting.

This is, in the end, the true genius of the bad-wheel grocery cart: it is not singular. It is a condition, not an individual. Somewhere in every store, at any given hour, on any given day, there is a cart with one bad wheel waiting in the corral for a shopper who looks like they are running slightly late. The cart has no opinion about who that shopper is. It will work with anyone. It is a democratic institution. It does not discriminate. It merely squeaks, and pulls left, and stops unexpectedly, and resumes squeaking, for everyone equally, without favor or preference, world without end.

It is a democratic institution. It does not discriminate. It merely squeaks, and pulls left, and stops unexpectedly, and resumes squeaking, for everyone equally, world without end.

The grocery cart with one bad wheel is not a problem waiting to be solved. It is a permanent feature of human experience, like the traffic jam and the printer that works fine until you have something urgent to print and the dentist appointment you have rescheduled three times. It is the universe reminding you, gently, via fluorescent-lit retail infrastructure, that control is temporary, plans are approximate, and the cherub is always, always, laughing.
