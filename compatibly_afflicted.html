Love in the Age of Asymbios

Asymbios is a dating site that matches people on their psychological conditions — phobias, manias, and documented compulsions. I went in skeptical. The algorithm had other ideas. This is my report.

By Nora Ellison  ·  Contributing Writer
Ms. Ellison has written for The Meridian Review, Broad Street Monthly, The Waverly Observer, and four other publications. She is currently based in Tucson.
Left to right: Franklin (arithmomaniac, Severity 9) conducts a preliminary ceiling assessment; Nora arrives already holding her coat; Todd presents his opening gift; Gary manages the balloon situation from a tactically sound position beneath the table. Gates A–B are visible in the background. Nora's flight departs at 6:15.

The assignment arrived on a Tuesday, forwarded from my editor at The Cassidy Journal, which had apparently picked it up from a tip submitted by a reader in Portland who had, himself, met his fiancée on the site in question. My editor's note read: "Two weeks. Full immersion. Try not to fall in love with a germaphobe." I told her I had no intention of falling in love with anyone. She said that was fine, and also that she needed it by the fifteenth, and also that I should know she was already "very excited about this one." I was less excited. I had a flight to catch.

Asymbios — named, its founder cheerfully confirms, after asymbiosophobia, the fear of incompatibility — launched in the spring of last year. It was founded by a man named Dr. Clement Purvis, whose credentials are listed on the site as "BSc (Psychology), MsC (Behavioral Pairing Sciences), and over a decade of experience in the emerging field of diagnostic compatibility." The field of diagnostic compatibility does not appear in any index I can locate. Dr. Purvis says this is because it is "nascent." I say this because I have now spent fourteen days on his website, which is more than enough time to have opinions about it.

Where Your Diagnosis Becomes Your Destiny

Most dating sites match you on interests, values, and attractiveness. We match you on something far more revealing: what you cannot help doing, and what you cannot bring yourself to face. Compatibility is not about who you want to be. It's about who you already, irreversibly, are.

Begin Your Assessment
By clicking "Begin Your Assessment," you confirm that your condition is documented, suspected, or strongly implied by the behavior of people who know you well. Asymbios does not provide clinical diagnosis. Asymbios is not responsible for outcomes. All matches are algorithmic and should be understood as probabilistic rather than prescriptive. Some conditions may worsen with proximity.

The registration process takes approximately forty minutes, which is longer than most dating sites but shorter than the site's own FAQ, which runs to eleven pages. You are asked to disclose your primary condition (phobia or mania), its severity on a scale of one to ten, any secondary conditions, and whether you have previously been in a relationship with someone whose condition was complementary, identical, or "actively antagonistic" to your own. There is a text field for elaboration. Most people elaborate at length. This is, perhaps, the site's most predictable feature: people with something to compulsively discuss have found a platform that rewards it.

Part I The Membership

The user base, at least as presented to me during my two weeks, sorted itself into recognizable types. The site's algorithm — called, with a confidence I admire, the Pairing Index — surfaces matches based on what Dr. Purvis describes as "dynamic complementarity," meaning the system attempts to find people whose conditions fit together usefully rather than catastrophically. In practice, this produces some genuinely interesting pairings and at least one that I am fairly certain is a crime.

Part II The Algorithm

Dr. Purvis agreed to a phone call on my fourth day on the site. He spoke for forty-seven minutes, of which perhaps twelve were directly responsive to my questions. He described the Pairing Index as "the first serious attempt to treat psychological conditions not as obstacles to love, but as the very architecture of it." He used the word "architecture" four times. I did not count them intentionally; I noticed afterward that I had written it down each time.

The algorithm, he explained, operates on three axes: complementarity (do your conditions fit together in a functional way), interference (would your conditions actively disrupt each other's expression), and escalation risk (would proximity to your match cause either condition to worsen over time). Scores on each axis combine into a Pairing Index score from zero to one hundred. He said most successful matches score between sixty-two and seventy-eight. He said anything above eighty was "an anomaly that warrants further study." He did not say what happened below sixty-two. I did not ask. I was distracted. I had a connection in Phoenix at 6:15 and needed to confirm my gate.

"We are not matching people on who they want to be. We are matching them on who they already, irreversibly, are. This is, statistically speaking, more honest than most relationships."

Part III The Research Profile

On day eight, I created a profile. This was, I want to be clear, strictly for journalistic purposes. You cannot fully understand a platform from the outside. This is basic methodology. My editor agreed, when I explained it to her, from the airport in Salt Lake City, where I had stopped briefly before continuing on to complete the assignment from Albuquerque, which has better light.

The registration questionnaire is, I will admit, surprisingly well-designed. It does not ask you to self-diagnose — it asks you to describe behaviors, tendencies, and what it gently calls "recurring patterns that others in your life have mentioned more than twice." I filled it out honestly, which took longer than I expected, partly because there were several questions I found myself wanting to revisit after initially moving past them, and partly because I had a connection at 7:40 and the airport WiFi was, as it always is in these situations, aspirationally named.

I will say, for the record, that the algorithm's assessment is not something I accept. The site's definition of dromomania — "a compulsive and irresistible urge to wander, characterized by an inability to remain in one place regardless of circumstance, relationship, or personal cost" — is a clinical description of a pathology. What I have is a professional disposition, a journalist's instinct, and a very reasonable preference for environments I haven't fully explored yet. These are different things. They are measurably different. I would explain precisely how they differ, but I am currently on a connecting flight to El Paso and the tray table is too small for the kind of thorough rebuttal this deserves.

Part IV The Replies

Within forty-eight hours of creating my research profile, I had received six messages. I reviewed each one carefully and, after consulting the Pairing Index assessments the algorithm appended to each inquiry, determined that none of them represented a viable match for the purposes of this article. My reasons were journalistic. The algorithm's reasons were its own.

I declined all six. My reasons, as I have noted, were journalistic. Wallace's message, I want to add, was not particularly well-written. The Denver/Salt Lake observation was imprecise — the terminals are, in fact, quite different — and the tone was self-congratulatory in a way I found unprofessional. These were my only reasons. I had a flight to catch.

A Note on the Founder

Dr. Clement Purvis, I discovered in my final days of research, has himself been a member of Asymbios since its launch. His listed condition is Onomatophobia — the fear of hearing one's own name spoken aloud. His profile photo shows a man with his back to the camera. His bio reads: "I built this site because I believe that what we cannot change about ourselves is not a flaw to be hidden. It is a fact to be disclosed, matched, and, where possible, loved." He has been on the site for fourteen months. He has not yet gone on a date. He says he is waiting for the algorithm to reach certainty. The algorithm, he notes, has never reached certainty about him. He considers this consistent with the science.

Conclusion Two Weeks. Some Notes.

Asymbios is, depending on your perspective, either a sincere attempt to reframe psychological conditions as romantic assets, or an extremely elaborate proof that self-knowledge and self-awareness are not the same thing. The members are, in the main, genuine. The algorithm is surprisingly thoughtful, even when it is wrong, which it is not always. The founder is a man who built a platform for radical honesty and has not yet been able to use it himself, which is either a tragedy or the most internally consistent thing I have encountered in years of reporting.

I closed my account on day fifteen. I did this from Phoenix, where I had stopped to catch a flight to somewhere I had not yet been. I had not planned to stop in Phoenix. The stopover was, as they often are, an opportunity I couldn't pass up.

The site sent me a closing survey with one final question: "Is there anything that prevented you from finding a compatible match?"

I left it blank. I had a gate to get to.
