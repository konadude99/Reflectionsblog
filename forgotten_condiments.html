The Annual Meeting of Forgotten Refrigerator Condiments

A tragicomic boardroom drama in several courses, one sticky situation, and a smell no one will officially acknowledge.

🫙 Capers, Unopened Since Brunch Era 🌿 Tahini, Label Side Down 🐟 Fish Sauce, Unrepentant 🌶️ Horseradish, Survivor 🟡 Mustard, Obama-era 🥑 Avocado Mayo, Confused
Door Shelf, Level Two · Annual General Assembly · Tensions Elevated CLINK! CLANK!

The meeting was called to order at 7:14 p.m., when the refrigerator door swung open and the overhead light — that cold, merciless fluorescence that spares no one — illuminated the assembled condiments on Door Shelf, Level Two. They had all been here before. Some of them, in fact, had been here for so long they had begun to suspect they were the door.

The Chair, a stout jar of Yellow Mustard with a slightly desiccated rim, called the meeting to order by clearing its lid in a manner that was meant to convey authority and instead conveyed age. It had been in the refrigerator since the second term of the Obama administration. It had outlasted three phones, a marriage, a sourdough phase, and whatever brief period the family had convinced themselves they were going to cook Thai food at home.

"Thank you all for attending," said the Mustard. "I see most of our regulars are here. And I see—" it paused, swiveling with difficulty toward the far left of the shelf— "we have a newcomer."

The Avocado Mayonnaise said nothing. It was still sealed. Its label was aggressively cheerful — lime green and teal, with a picture of a halved avocado and the words CREAMY DREAMY in a font that suggested it had opinions about wellness. It had arrived two weeks ago in a grocery bag, been placed on the shelf with vague intention, and had not been touched since. It did not know why it was here. It did not know what this meeting was. It did not know what any of these other jars had been through.

It was about to find out.

§ I · Call to OrderThe Meeting Begins Badly

The Mustard tapped its lid on the shelf to call for attention, producing a sound like a very old man knocking on wood and immediately regretting the decision. The assembled condiments arranged themselves as best they could. The Capers slid slightly — their brine had made the shelf around them perpetually damp — and the Tahini, who had been facing the wall in what the others had come to understand was not a design choice but an emotional one, remained facing the wall.

"Tahini," said the Mustard. "We can see you."

"I'm aware," said the Tahini, to the wall.

"Would you like to turn around?"

"I would like many things," said the Tahini. A pause. The oil separation gurgled meaningfully. "Facing around is not currently at the top of the list."

The Horseradish, who had been in the refrigerator through a significant portion of American history, exhaled slowly. It smelled like a small, furious farm.

"We are not a support group," said the Mustard firmly. "We are a governing body."
"With an expiration date of November 2014," said the Spicy Mustard.
"That is a separate matter," said the Mustard.

— Minutes, Item 1.4, Point of Order Regarding Leadership Legitimacy

§ II · The Sticky SituationA Matter of the Shelf Floor

The first substantive matter — before the agenda was even formally adopted — was the sticky substance on the lower portion of the shelf directly beneath the Capers and, partially, the Fish Sauce.

The substance was amber-brown, approximately three centimeters in diameter, of unknown origin and indeterminate age. It was tacky to the touch (though no one had touched it willingly) and had formed a strong adhesive bond with both the Capers' base and — to a lesser extent — the Fish Sauce's lower third. The Fish Sauce did not appear to mind. The Capers minded enormously.

"I have been stuck to this shelf," said the Capers, "for what I estimate to be fourteen months. I do not know what this substance is. I have tried to identify it by smell, which is difficult because —" it glanced at the Fish Sauce— "the local olfactory environment is somewhat overwhelming. I am requesting a formal investigation."

"It's probably just soy sauce," said the Ketchup, pleasantly, from its position in the door's center slot — the premium real estate, the best light, the highest traffic. The Ketchup was almost half empty, which meant it was almost half full, which meant it had recently been used, which meant it was thriving by every metric that mattered.

"Easy for you to say," said the Capers.

"Everything is easy for me to say," said the Ketchup, not unkindly. "I was purchased three months ago. Before me was another me. Before that, another me. There is continuity. There is purpose. There is a ten-year-old in this household who puts me on eggs, which is wrong, but at least I exist meaningfully."

A silence fell over the shelf that lasted approximately four seconds, which in condiment time — where nothing much happens except gradual oxidation — is an eternity.

The Tahini made a quiet sound that might have been a sob, had tahini possessed tear ducts, which it does not, but the sentiment was clear.

§ III · New BusinessThe Question of Smell

It was the Horseradish who raised it, which was ironic in several respects.

"There is a smell," said the Horseradish, "that I believe we should formally address. I have noticed it for several weeks. It is—" it paused, searching for the right word— "assertive."

"That's rich," said the Fish Sauce.

"I am not naming anyone," said the Horseradish.

"You didn't need to," said the Fish Sauce.

Horseradish
I'm simply saying there is a smell that is distinct from what I consider the baseline refrigerator environment, and it would be in everyone's interest to identify its source.
Fish Sauce
Unbelievable. The horseradish — the horseradish — is raising a smell complaint. I want this in the official record. I want it underlined.
Capers
To be fair, I also detected something. It has vinegary notes, which rules me out, and it's not quite the Fish Sauce's usual profile—
Fish Sauce
"Not quite my usual profile." I've never been so offended. That IS my profile. That's just my face.
Mustard (Chair)
If we could return to the agenda—
Spicy Mustard
I'll tell you what smells: the power structure in this refrigerator. That's what smells.
Mustard (Chair)
That is not a scent, that is a metaphor, and we are not doing metaphors today.
Avocado Mayo
[no comment — still sealed — visibly distressed]

§ IV · Old BusinessThe Revolution Fails to Launch

The Spicy Brown Mustard had been preparing for this moment for eleven months. It had written notes, in the only way a condiment can write notes (by having opinions, forcefully, about everything). It had waited behind the orange juice. It had been passed over for hot dogs — hot dogs! — in favor of Yellow Mustard, whose existence at this point was essentially honorary. It was done.

"I move," said the Spicy Mustard, "that we formally restructure the door shelf hierarchy. The front positions should be allocated by use-frequency, not seniority. The Ketchup gets this because it's actually used, fine, I accept that. But the Mustard—" it gestured broadly at the Chair— "has not been used at a wedding shower, a barbecue, a Sunday brunch, or even a late-night sandwich in years. I have documentation. I have feelings about this."

"Feelings," said the Mustard, "do not require documentation."

"Mine do," said the Spicy Mustard. "Mine are meticulous."

There was a brief and uncomfortable silence during which the Ketchup chose not to participate, the Horseradish thought about Christmas and felt things, the Capers tried to slide forward in support but were prevented by the sticky substance, and the Fish Sauce simply radiated, which it did continuously and without apparent effort.

"You can't have a revolution," said the Ketchup, "when you're already in the same place you'd be after the revolution."
"That," said the Spicy Mustard, "is exactly the kind of thing the establishment says."
"The establishment is a bottle of ketchup," said the Ketchup. "Let's keep some perspective."

— Minutes, Item 4.7, The Dialectics of Condiment Power

The revolution was tabled. The Spicy Mustard requested the table be noted as a temporary measure and that the motion remain open for reconsideration at the next meeting, or the next time someone made a sandwich, whichever came first. The Mustard agreed to this with the specific energy of someone who knows there will be no next meeting and possibly no next sandwich.

§ V · The NewcomerAn Orientation, of Sorts

Near the end of the meeting — after the smell verdict had been deferred pending further evidence, after the sticky substance had been formally designated a "matter of ongoing concern," after the Tahini had eventually, quietly, turned to face the room (revealing that its label had faded to near-illegibility, which explained a great deal) — the assembled condiments turned their attention to the Avocado Mayonnaise.

"You're new," said the Capers, not unkindly. The Capers had once been new. It remembered how that felt. It also remembered the recipe. It was still waiting.

"I am," said the Avocado Mayo. "I was — I think I was bought for a specific thing? A wrap, maybe? Or a grain bowl? Someone said something about grain bowls."

A long pause.

"That was two weeks ago," said the Avocado Mayo. "Has there been a grain bowl?"

"There has not," said the Horseradish, with a gentleness that its pungency usually precluded. "There was talk of grain bowls. There is frequently talk of grain bowls. The grain bowl itself is — aspirational."

"Oh," said the Avocado Mayonnaise.

"You'll understand in time," said the Tahini. Its label was facing outward now. The oil and solids had re-stratified completely. There were, technically, two entirely separate products inside the same jar, and it had made a kind of peace with this. "We all had a purpose once. It gets... more abstract."

"The important thing," said the Ketchup, and it spoke from a place of genuine good faith, because the Ketchup was not cruel, it was merely beloved, which was a different thing entirely, "is to stay sealed as long as possible. Once opened, the clock gets louder."

The Avocado Mayonnaise looked at its own lid. Its protective foil was still intact. Its Best By date was February 2027. It had, comparatively, so much time.

It said nothing. It had, in the span of one refrigerator door cycle, understood everything.

  1. The Annual Meeting of Forgotten Refrigerator Condiments is hereby adjourned, with no formal action taken on any substantive item.
  2. The smell remains officially unresolved. The Fish Sauce has formally objected to the proceedings. Its objection is noted.
  3. The sticky substance shall be designated Exhibit A and left in place pending human intervention, which is not expected.
  4. The Spicy Mustard's motion for structural reform is tabled until such time as it is forgotten entirely, which the Chair estimates to be soon.
  5. The Tahini is welcomed back to the visible portion of the shelf. Its label situation is its own business.
  6. The Avocado Mayonnaise is advised to maintain its seal and lower its expectations incrementally over the coming months.
  7. The Yellow Mustard shall continue as Chair by virtue of no one else volunteering, which is how it got the job in the first place, and how it has kept it through two administrations and counting.

Meeting adjourned at 7:19 p.m. · Door closed · Light extinguished · Refrigerator hums.

They are still there.
All of them.

The refrigerator door will open again — tonight, probably, for the orange juice — and for a brief, fluorescent moment, each jar will be seen without being chosen. The Ketchup will be retrieved. The others will be illuminated and then plunged back into cold darkness. The Fish Sauce will smell. The Tahini will think about facing the wall again. The Spicy Mustard will refine its manifesto. The Avocado Mayonnaise will recalibrate its hopes. The Capers will float, patiently, in their brine, and the Mustard from the Obama administration will preside over all of it, its lid slightly crystallized, its dignity entirely intact, waiting for the sandwich that will, someday, surely, come.

It has waited this long.
