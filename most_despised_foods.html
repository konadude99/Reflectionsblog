Enemies of the Plate:
America's Most Despised Foods

A hard-hitting, stomach-turning exposé into the foods that make an entire nation gag — featuring real human reactions, damning survey data, and a Barf Factor™ rating for every offender.

By Our Staff Correspondent, Who Now Regrets Eating for Research  ·  2025

There exists, in the vast and sunlit landscape of American cuisine, a shadow world — a culinary underworld of textures that slither, smells that linger long after the plate has been cleared, and flavors so aggressively divisive that they have torn apart friendships, ruined Thanksgiving dinners, and sent more than one grown adult fleeing the table in tears. This is their story.

In 2025, YouGov surveyed 2,239 Americans about which normal, grocery-store-available foods they simply could not abide. The results were, frankly, shocking — not because the foods on the list were exotic, but because so many of them are things your grandmother probably served with a smile and the words, "You'll love it, trust me."

Spoiler: we did not love it.

Man on the Street — Union Square, New York City
Gerald, 54, retired plumber
"Liver. Just liver. I've been avoiding it since 1987 and I plan to continue through the afterlife."

Anchovies
56% dislike · America's #1 Enemy

The most hated food in America when you count actual "hate" votes. Combines a metallic taste, a grainy-yet-dense texture, and the distinct sensory experience of eating someone's feelings. A classic childhood trauma vehicle.

§ 4 — The Usual SuspectsThe Categories of Culinary Contempt

Across every survey conducted in recent years, hated foods fall reliably into a few camps — almost as if human disgust has its own organizational chart.

Fishy and briny foods top every list, punished for their smell, their saltiness, and their habit of tasting exactly like you fell into the sea. Organ meats combine metallic flavors, dense textures, and what researchers delicately call "childhood dinner trauma." Bitter vegetables are hated by those genetically predisposed to detect certain bitter compounds more intensely — yes, this is a real thing, and it's why some people are completely unbothered by Brussels sprouts while others act like they've been asked to eat a pine cone. And fermented and funky foods — the blue cheeses, the capers, the olives — split opinion straight down the middle. People who love them experience a rich complexity; people who hate them experience someone accidentally left the food out too long.

Man on the Street — Outside Whole Foods, Boston
Marcus, 33, finance professional
"Look, I eat well. I know blue cheese is 'sophisticated.' But when I open it in the office fridge, three colleagues have filed HR complaints. That's not sophistication. That's a hazmat situation."

§ 5 — Honorary MentionThe Rhubarb Situation

Rhubarb — a stringy, aggressively tart stalk that botanically speaking is a vegetable but culturally insists on being served as pie — does not quite crack the top-ten hated foods in national surveys. This is less because people like it, and more because most people have simply chosen not to think about it.

It has, however, all the hallmarks of a deeply suspicious food: it is fibrous, it requires industrial quantities of sugar to be edible, and the leaves are actually poisonous, which seems like relevant information that rhubarb should have led with.

Man on the Street — A Farmers Market, Vermont
Janet, 58, librarian
"I don't dislike rhubarb, exactly. I just think of it as nature's way of saying 'you really needed that sugar, not me.' The plant is just a delivery vehicle for sugar. It's honest about it. I respect that."

§ 6 — ConclusionsWhat This All Means for Humanity

After extensive field research, several man-on-the-street interviews, and one regrettable anchovy tasting session in the office, we can report the following findings:

First: Liver is objectively, statistically, the single most hated food in America — not just the most disliked, but the most actively loathed, with 40% of Americans saying they hate it with specific, personal intensity. It has earned this distinction through decades of unwavering commitment to metallic flavor and traumatic school-night dinners.

Second: Anchovies are the most widely disliked food, meaning more people have an opinion about them — almost certainly because someone keeps putting them on the pizza.

Third: The categories of hated food have remained remarkably consistent across surveys and across decades. We as a species have apparently always recoiled from things that smell strongly of the sea, have been exposed to controlled rot, or once constituted the inside of an animal. This is, some would argue, wisdom.

And finally: if you are someone who genuinely loves liver, chitterlings, and anchovies on your oyster-topped blue cheese pizza — first, we respect you. Second, please do not tell us. We're still recovering from the research phase.

"There are no bad foods. Just foods that most people will not eat for any amount of money."
