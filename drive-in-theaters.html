Somewhere in New Jersey in 1933, a man named Richard Hollingshead nailed a screen to some trees in his backyard, plopped a projector on his car hood, and pointed it at the darkness. His mother found indoor theater seats uncomfortable. His solution: don't go inside. Build the theater around the car. Let everyone stay exactly where they are.

And thus, one of the most magnificently American ideas in the history of entertainment was born—not from corporate vision boards or Silicon Valley pivots, but from a son's maternal devotion and a backyard full of questionable engineering decisions.

The First Drive-In, By the Numbers: One determined son. One uncomfortable mother. One projector balanced on a car hood. Several radios behind windshields. An unknowable quantity of bugs.
🌙

From Backyard to Bonanza: A Brief and Magnificent Rise

Hollingshead's patent turned into a national obsession. Drive-ins spread like kudzu across the American landscape through the 1940s and 50s, perfectly timed with two forces of nature: postwar car ownership and suburban expansion. America had cars. America had land. America had an itch it desperately needed to scratch, and that itch was: watching movies while sitting in a 1957 Chevrolet eating a hot dog.

At its peak, the drive-in wasn't a novelty—it was infrastructure. The All-Weather Drive-In in Copiague, New York, could hold over 2,500 cars. That's essentially a temporary city of idling engines and popcorn. A city with one movie theater and absolutely no parking problems, because every single resident was a parking spot.

The Operational Chaos Nobody Warned You About

Running a drive-in theater looks, from the outside, deceptively simple: put up a screen, show a movie, collect money. The reality was a rich tapestry of operational nightmares that could only exist in a venue where the audience arrives in several tons of automobile each night.

The Heel Problem

In summer heat, women walking to concession stands would sink into soft asphalt in high heels. Some theaters handed out wooden boards. Others just quietly rethought their entire surface material.

Mosquito Warfare

In the South and Midwest, drive-ins waged perpetual insect warfare. Pre-show fogging systems sprayed insecticide across the lot. Patrons watched cinema through a haze of bug spray and existential dread.

The Speaker Heist Era

Those little metal speakers clipped to car windows? People stole them. Constantly. Compulsively. Theaters had to chain them to poles. It remains one of history's most inexplicable theft epidemics.

Trunk Stowaways

Some theaters charged per car. Teenagers, being teenagers, crammed absurd numbers of people into trunks to save money. Employees began checking trunks. The discoveries were, we can only assume, varied.

Daylight Disaster

Some operators tried daytime screenings with bigger projectors. The result: washed-out images, squinting audiences, and the discovery that sunlight is, in fact, undefeated. The experiments mostly stopped.

Wildlife Concessions

Raccoons, seagulls, and other opportunists discovered that drive-in concession areas were essentially open buffets. The negotiated détente between theaters and local fauna remains unwritten but universally respected.

Fun Fact: Drive-ins didn't really make money on tickets. Film distributors took enormous cuts. The real profit? Concessions. This is why bringing your own food was treated roughly on par with grand theft auto.
💘

The "Passion Pit": A Name That Required Zero Explanation

By the 1950s and 60s, drive-ins had acquired a nickname: the passion pit. It was not subtle. It required no footnotes. Everyone immediately understood exactly what it meant and exactly what was being referred to.

Local police occasionally swept lots with flashlights, in what must have been an extraordinarily awkward beat to be assigned. Some theaters tried to combat the phenomenon by angling the little metal speakers outward so employees could monitor whether cars were, in fact, watching the movie. They were often not watching the movie.

And yet—simultaneously—families also loved drive-ins, for entirely different but equally practical reasons:

The Great Dual Nature of the Drive-In: For teenagers, it was privacy. For parents, it was logistics. Kids could play on the playgrounds directly beneath the giant screen until dark, then fall asleep in the backseat while parents watched the film in peace. Nobody had to be quiet. Babies could cry. It was, functionally, the most humane cinema experience ever devised.

Drive-ins succeeded because they did something truly radical: they reduced behavioral friction. You didn't have to shush your children, hide your snacks, or pretend you weren't constantly shifting in an increasingly uncomfortable seat. You sat in your car, in your own climate, with your own food, surrounded by your own mess. Cinema, finally, as God intended.

The Long Slide Into Darkness (And Not the Fun Kind)

Television gets blamed for killing the drive-in. This is roughly like blaming a single raindrop for a flood. Yes, it contributed. But the drive-in's decline was a multi-instrument symphony of unfortunate economics.

The Land Problem

Drive-ins require enormous plots of land. As suburban land values rose, operators faced a simple, merciless equation: the land is worth more than the business sitting on it. Selling to a developer for a shopping center? Suddenly a lot more appealing than another summer of mosquito fogging and speaker theft prevention.

The Idle Asset Problem

Unlike indoor theaters, which can run films morning through night 365 days a year, drive-ins sit completely dark and idle all day. Every sunny afternoon is a plot of expensive land generating exactly zero revenue. Business schools have a term for this. It is not flattering.

The Digital Upgrade Problem

When the film industry switched to digital projection in the 2000s and 2010s, the cost of upgrading each screen ran between $70,000 and $100,000. For small, family-run drive-ins operating on thin seasonal margins, this was often simply the end of the road.

Everything Else

Cable TV. VHS. DVD. Streaming. The entire home entertainment revolution, one relentless format at a time, chipping away at the novelty. And in cold climates, seasonal operations meant operators had four good months to make twelve months of profit. The math, as they say, does not math.

The Survivors: A Field Guide to Drive-Ins Still Standing

The few hundred drive-ins still operating fall into beautifully distinct personality types, each having evolved a unique strategy for not dying. Observe:

🕰️

The Traditionalists

Places like Mesa Drive-In in Pueblo, Colorado—simple, community-focused, largely unchanged from 1955. Their competitive advantage is being aggressively, defiantly normal. And it works.

🍺

The Reinventors

The Coyote Drive-In in Fort Worth added food trucks, craft bars, and lawn seating. The movie is almost beside the point. It's now a social venue that happens to also show films. Clever.

🎞️

The Purists

Pennsylvania's Mahoning Drive-In shows only 35mm film prints. It has become a pilgrimage site for cinephiles who consider digital projection a form of moral failure.

And Then There Are the Wonderfully Eccentric Ones

🪖 Bengies Drive-In, Maryland: Rules Are Law

  • → Bengies operates with near-military precision. The rules are extensive, specific, and non-negotiable.
  • → Triple features are standard. You will watch all three films. There is no alternative.
  • → Outside food policies are enforced with the enthusiasm of a border crossing.
  • → The result is a genuinely beloved institution. Turns out people appreciate knowing exactly where they stand.

Then there's Mission Tiki in Montclair, California, which leans fully into tropical kitsch—tiki torches, themed decorations, the general vibe that you're watching a movie at a luau. And Wellfleet Drive-In in Massachusetts doubles as a flea market, occasionally combined with wildlife encounters, because apparently this is what "differentiation" looks like on Cape Cod.

And let us not forget Shankweiler's Drive-In in Pennsylvania, which claims to be the oldest operating drive-in in America—open since 1934. It has been operating for over ninety years. Whatever they're doing, it works.

COVID's Accidental Gift to Drive-In Cinema

In early 2020, as the world shut down and indoor gatherings became a public health hazard, someone had a realization: we have hundreds of venues where people watch movies from inside their own private sealed metal boxes.

Drive-ins surged. Events were held in drive-in parking lots. Church services were broadcast from drive-in speakers. Concert livestreams flickered across drive-in screens. The format that had been slowly dying for fifty years suddenly proved itself uniquely suited to a global pandemic—a sentence that belongs in the lexicon of history's most unexpected plot twists.

"The drive-in was designed for exactly this situation," said nobody in 1933, because in 1933 there was no situation like this. And yet, here we were.

Why, After Everything, We Still Love Them

The drive-in theater survives not because it's the most efficient way to watch a film—it is demonstrably, comically not—but because it offers something modern cinema cannot replicate:

Night air. Dashboard glow. The distant, slightly tinny sound of a movie playing through a speaker clipped to your window. Stars overhead. Popcorn that you may or may not have smuggled in your coat. The peculiar communal intimacy of a thousand people in separate cars all watching the same screen, each in their own world, each briefly part of something larger.

It is, improbably, both the most private and the most communal entertainment experience available. You are alone together. You are watching as a crowd but feeling it alone. This is not a thing indoor theaters do, or streaming, or anything else in the entertainment universe.

Drive-ins didn't fail because people stopped loving them. They failed because land got expensive, technology changed, and their social role was replaced. What remains is something filtered and true—surviving only where it can differentiate, modernize, or simply operate where the economics permit. They are, in that sense, not relics. They're case studies in the extraordinary stubborn persistence of a genuinely good idea.

Richard Hollingshead nailed a screen to some trees for his mother.

Ninety years later, people are still parking their cars in fields and looking at the dark in the best possible way.

Some ideas, it turns out, just have very good bones.
