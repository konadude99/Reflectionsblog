What We Lose When Local
Newspapers Disappear

Not just journalism, but memory, accountability, community identity — and a shared record of what actually happened here.

Section I

The Numbers Behind the Silence

The erosion has been breathtaking in its speed and scope. In 2005, the United States had roughly 7,300 local newspapers — dailies and weeklies serving towns, counties, and neighborhoods. By 2025, that number had fallen to about 4,500. The Medill Local News Initiative at Northwestern University estimates that at the current pace, the country could lose another third by 2030.

The financial cause of death is almost always the same: the collapse of print advertising revenue. For most of the 20th century, local newspapers held a near-monopoly on the most valuable forms of local advertising — classified ads, legal notices, grocery inserts, auto listings, real estate. Digital platforms — first Craigslist, then Google, then Facebook — stripped each of those revenue streams away in turn. Between 2000 and 2022, newspaper print advertising revenue fell from roughly $48 billion to under $7 billion, an 85% decline in just over two decades.

Newsrooms that survived the 2008–2009 recession were often left with skeleton staffs. Then COVID-19 hit in 2020, eliminating the local event listings, display ads, and restaurant advertisements that propped up many of the weakest survivors. The Poynter Institute estimated that more than 100 local newsrooms closed permanently in the first year of the pandemic alone.

Private equity’s arrival added another dimension to the story. Companies like Alden Global Capital bought up chains of local papers and aggressively cut costs — slashing reporting staff, selling buildings, and sometimes reducing papers from daily to weekly publication before closing them entirely. Researchers at the University of North Carolina found that Alden-owned newspapers cut newsroom staff at roughly twice the rate of comparable papers, in some cases leaving “ghost papers” that published but no longer truly covered their communities.

“When a local newspaper closes, a community loses not just a source of news — it loses its collective memory, its watchdog, and its public square, all at once.”
Section II

What Was Actually in That Paper

To understand what disappears, it helps to remember what a local newspaper actually was. Not just political coverage and investigative reporting — though those mattered enormously — but an entire ecosystem of community information that had no other home.

⚾
High School Sports

Box scores from Friday night football. Swimming meet results. The JV volleyball schedule. For student athletes in small towns, the local paper was often the only record that they ever played, competed, or won.

🏫
School Board Coverage

The local reporter who attended every school board meeting, budget hearing, and curriculum debate. When they vanish, those meetings continue — but in the dark. Decisions affecting thousands of children happen without a single witness from the press.

📖
Obituaries

The most-read section in most small-town papers. A well-written obituary said: this person existed, mattered, and was loved. For many families, it was the permanent public record of a life — clipped and saved in family Bibles.

📋
Want Ads & Classifieds

Jobs at the hardware store. The used truck for sale. The babysitter looking for work. Craigslist theoretically replaced this, but it serves a regional audience, and requires internet access that not all rural residents have.

✏
The Daily Crossword & Comics

Trivial? Perhaps. But the comics page and the crossword were what kept some subscribers subscribing. The ritual of sitting with the paper and a pencil is one the digital age has struggled to replicate.

🎪
Arts & Entertainment Listings

The community theater production. The church concert. The art fair. The farmers’ market hours. These events didn’t disappear when the paper did — but awareness of them did, and attendance often followed.

⛪
Church Bulletins & Civic Notices

Notices of town hall meetings. Zoning hearings. Library hours. Volunteer opportunities. The paper was the low-tech public square that told residents what was happening in the life of their community.

🏪
Local Business Ads

The hardware store’s holiday sale. The diner’s new lunch special. Local advertising was the economic symbiosis that kept both businesses and the newspaper alive — and signaled to readers that Main Street was open.

Section III

The Obituary Problem: When Lives Go Unrecorded

Obituaries may seem like a minor concern against the sweep of democratic accountability and investigative journalism. But ask a family in a town that lost its paper where they will publish their mother’s obituary, and the human cost becomes immediate.

Obituaries: A Disappearing Record

For most of American history, the local newspaper obit was the one place where an ordinary person’s life was formally acknowledged in print — their service, their family, their work, their faith. It was searchable. It was archived. It was the permanent civic record of a human life.

Funeral homes now often post digital obituaries on their own websites, but these pages are frequently taken down after a few years. There is no newspaper archive, no microfilm, no permanent civic record. In towns without papers, entire generations are beginning to disappear from the public record within a decade of dying.

Genealogists, historians, and researchers have long relied on newspaper obituary archives to reconstruct community history. The loss of those archives — and the cessation of new obits — is a slow catastrophe for local memory that will not be fully felt for another generation.

Section IV

Accountability in the Dark: What Happens When No One Watches

The research on what happens when local news disappears is, at this point, extensive and damning. Across multiple peer-reviewed studies, the loss of local journalism correlates with measurable changes in how governments behave when no one is watching.

A landmark 2018 study by economists at the University of Notre Dame found that communities that lost their local newspaper saw a significant increase in municipal borrowing costs — because without local coverage, bond markets had less information about how well the government was managing public finances. Corruption and mismanagement, it turns out, are easier to detect when someone is paying attention.

Multiple studies have found that voter turnout in local elections drops in communities where newspapers close. Civic engagement is partly a function of civic information — and when the information disappears, so does some of the participation.

Research from Penelope Muse Abernathy at the University of North Carolina documented case after case of local corruption, misuse of public funds, and environmental violations that were discovered only because a local reporter was there, attending tedious meetings and reading dense budget documents. Without that reporter, many of those stories never surface.

“Towns without newspapers have higher rates of government debt, lower voter turnout, and more officials who run unopposed. Democracy, it turns out, needs a witness.”
Section V

News Deserts: Where Communities Go Dark

A “news desert” is defined as a county with no local newspaper and no other significant local news outlet. As of 2025, researchers at Northwestern University’s Medill School count more than 200 counties that meet this definition. Together with more than 1,500 counties that have only a single local news source remaining, roughly 50 million Americans have little or no access to reliable local news.

These are not just rural backwaters. News deserts exist in suburban counties, in mid-sized cities that lost their second or third paper, and in urban neighborhoods that never had a paper focused on their community. They are disproportionately lower-income, disproportionately communities of color, and disproportionately places already struggling economically.

A further 1,500+ counties have only a single local news outlet remaining — often a weekly with a part-time staff of two or three people covering a county of 30,000 or 50,000 residents. These are papers one bad year away from joining the desert.

Section VI

The Loss of a Shared Story

Beyond the measurable — the elections, the bond rates, the corruption — there is something harder to quantify but perhaps more fundamental: the loss of the shared story that makes a community feel like a community.

Social scientists have long understood that one of the things that binds a community together is a shared information environment — a set of common facts, common concerns, and common references. The local newspaper was, for most of the 20th century, the primary institution that created and maintained that environment. It told you what your neighbors were doing, what your schools were teaching, what your government was debating, and who among you had just died or just been born.

Research by the Knight Foundation has found consistent links between local news consumption and measures of civic participation: volunteering, attending community meetings, knowing neighbors by name, donating to local causes. When news consumption declines, those behaviors tend to follow — not dramatically overnight, but gradually, like water leaving a landscape.

There is also the phenomenon researchers call “community cohesion loss” — the feeling, reported by residents of news deserts in study after study, that the community feels less like a community. People don’t know what’s happening at city hall. They don’t know the high school football scores. They don’t know that the diner they always meant to visit is closing next month. The invisible threads that made a town feel inhabited by people who knew each other begin to fray.

The Polarization Connection

Several researchers have linked the collapse of local news to rising political polarization. When there is no shared local information environment, people retreat to national media — cable news, partisan websites, social media — which are optimized not for community coherence but for national conflict. A 2022 study in the American Political Science Review found that the loss of local newspapers was associated with increased straight-ticket partisan voting and a decline in split-ticket voting that traditionally characterized local races. National politics floods into the vacuum that local news leaves behind.

Section VII

What Is Rising in the Ruins

The picture is not entirely bleak. Across the country, a scattered but real movement is attempting to fill the gap that commercial newspapers have left behind.

Nonprofit local newsrooms have become the most significant structural innovation in local journalism. Organizations like The Texas Tribune, The Colorado Sun, Mississippi Today, and hundreds of smaller outlets operate on reader donations, foundation grants, and membership models. The Institute for Nonprofit News counted more than 400 nonprofit news organizations in the United States in 2024, up from fewer than 100 a decade earlier.

University-affiliated journalism programs have stepped in to cover communities in their regions, training students while providing genuine local coverage. The Howard Center for Investigative Journalism, the Medill Chicago Bureau, and dozens of state university programs now function as de facto local newsrooms in places where commercial journalism has withdrawn.

Community news sites and hyper-local newsletters — often run by one or two journalists who left legacy media — now serve many small towns. Platforms like Substack have made it possible for a single reporter to build a paid subscriber base large enough to sustain a living. These outlets rarely have the resources of the papers they replaced, but they are present, they are watchful, and in many cases they are doing work that would otherwise not be done at all.

Still, for every success story there are dozens of communities where no replacement has arrived. The Knight Foundation, MacArthur Foundation, and others have invested hundreds of millions of dollars in local news sustainability over the past decade — with results that remain encouraging but partial. The scale of the loss continues to outpace the scale of the response.

Closing Reflection

The Couple on the Bench

Look again at the photograph at the top of this page. An older man in a tweed jacket, deep in The Daily Herald. A woman in a floral dress beside him, absorbed in The Morning Chronicle. Sparrows at their feet. Lilacs overhead. The boulangerie’s chalkboard promising fresh bread and pastries, daily.

What are they reading? Probably the local scores from last night’s game. The notice about the library book sale. A letter to the editor about the stop sign at Elm and Fourth. An obituary for someone they half-knew. The crossword, saved for the walk home.

They are not reading about the war in some distant country or the machinations of the national legislature. They are reading about here. They are reading about the place where they live, and in doing so, they are participating in the invisible civic ritual that keeps a place coherent — that turns a collection of houses and streets into something that can honestly be called a community.

That ritual is ending. Not everywhere, not forever, and not without resistance. But faster than we have recognized, and more consequentially than we have yet reckoned with. Two newspapers a week, closing their doors, taking their archives with them, leaving communities a little more alone in the dark.

The morning paper was never just a product. It was a promise — that someone was paying attention, that the story of this place was being recorded, that you and your neighbors existed in a shared and legible world. When it is gone, something goes with it that no algorithm, no social media feed, and no national broadcast has yet figured out how to replace.
