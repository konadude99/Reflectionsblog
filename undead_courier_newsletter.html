Exclusive Investigation

The Odor From Sector 4 Has Been Identified;
Winchell Issues Non-Apology

By our Olfactory Correspondent  ·  Breaking Developments Desk

Following three weeks of community complaints, a formal odor committee investigation, and two strongly-worded letters slipped under the door of Unit 7 at the Shady Rest Undead Community, the source of the smell that residents had attributed to Gerald Winchell has been conclusively identified.

It is not Winchell.

According to the Odor Committee's final report, the smell emanates from a broken chest freezer in Winchell's garage, where he had been secretly storing "a substantial collection of roadkill gathered over a period the committee estimates at four to six months." The collection included, but was not limited to, three opossums, an indeterminate number of raccoons, what appears to have been a very large groundhog, and something the committee's report describes only as "regional in character and persistent."

Winchell, reached for comment while eating something in his front yard, said the collection represented "personal food stores" and that the freezer "was working fine until it wasn't." He acknowledged he should have mentioned the freezer. He did not apologize for the smell. He noted that he had been "meaning to get to it."

The committee has requested that Winchell remove or consume the contents of the freezer within two weeks. Winchell said he would "try to get to it." The committee has accepted this as a commitment.

Residents are reminded that community freezer storage policies are listed in the Shady Rest Homeowner's Handbook, page 12, under the heading "Hoarding of Perishables and Semi-Perishables." Winchell has acknowledged he has not read page 12. He has not read any page. He does not remember where he put the handbook.

Feature · The Great Mobility Debate

Fast or Slow? The Question That Has Divided Our Community for Generations

By Dr. Reginald Fester, D.Z. (Doctor of Zombology) · Mobility & Locomotion Desk

It is the question that arises at every community meeting, invariably derails the agenda, and has on at least two occasions resulted in a slow-motion altercation that lasted eleven days. Are fast zombies or slow zombies better? And more to the point: which are you, and are you doing it right?

Your Courier has conducted an in-depth investigation, which is to say we shuffled over to several community members and asked them how they felt about their speed, and then shuffled back and wrote down what they said, which took most of the month.

The Case for Slow. The traditionalists — led, inevitably, by Mildred Groan of Pinecrest Lane — argue that slowness is not a flaw but a philosophy. "Patience," Groan told this reporter, in a conversation that took approximately four hours and covered her position in what she describes as "necessary depth." "We don't chase. We accumulate. Eventually everything comes to us." The slow faction points out that their approach conserves energy, reduces limb-loss through overexertion, and has a certain dignified quality that the fast crowd, frankly, lacks. "Have you seen them?" asked Groan. "Running everywhere. Limbs flying. It's embarrassing. We didn't survive death to embarrass ourselves."

The Case for Fast. The fast contingent, represented here by Derek Scramble, who moved to the community recently and immediately ruffled feathers by sprinting to the community mailbox, argues that speed is simply more effective. "Results," said Scramble, who was difficult to interview because he kept starting sentences before finishing them and moving toward things that weren't there. "It's about results. You see something, you go get it. Why wait? We have all of eternity but also we don't, because eventually the tendons — " He moved away before finishing the sentence. He had not been invited to return.

The Science. Research conducted by this newsletter's Science Department (which is this reporter standing near the community bulletin board) suggests that speed may correlate with recency of reanimation. Newer arrivals tend to move faster. Long-term community members tend to have settled into a more contemplative pace. Whether this represents a maturation of zombie philosophy or simply accumulated joint deterioration is a matter of ongoing debate.

The community's position, as voted on at the last meeting (quorum: 9 out of 47 registered members; 6 of the 9 voted "unh"), remains officially neutral. The Courier recommends each community member move at whatever pace feels natural, provided they are not moving natural in the direction of the freezer in Winchell's garage.

Style & Grooming

This Season in Zombie Fashion: What Works, What Doesn't, and What Has Fallen Off

By our Style Correspondent, whose name we have misplaced

Fashion, as this community knows, presents unique challenges. The standard considerations — fit, color, appropriateness to occasion — remain relevant, but must be weighed against the more pressing concerns of durability, exposure tolerance, and the management of the inevitable. This season's report covers the key trends and offers practical guidance.

The consensus this fall is that earth tones continue to be the most practical choice, as they tend to conceal evidence of recent activity and blend with the natural environments in which our community spends much of its time. Olive green, clay brown, and what the fashion community is calling "grave grey" are all acceptable. White is strongly discouraged. White was a mistake before reanimation and it remains a mistake after.

Regarding footwear: The community has, after extensive debate at the August meeting, reached a consensus that footwear is optional but recommended for members whose mobility has not yet been significantly affected. Those who have progressed beyond the point where footwear is practical are advised to simply proceed without comment. The committee notes that crawling is always an option and should not be treated as a source of embarrassment.

Formal wear has seen a resurgence, largely due to the fact that several new community members arrived in formal attire and have declined to change. The committee is not opposed to this. A tasteful blazer, even in the current condition of most blazers in the community, conveys a sense of occasion that speaks well of us all.

Obituaries

Those Who Have Passed a Second Time

The Undead Courier honors community members who have completed their second and, presumably, final departure. Submissions should be legible.

Gerald "Fish" Fishman
Born: 1944 · First Departure: 2019 · Second Departure: Recent
Gerald was a longtime community member, former regional sales manager, and the founder of the Shady Rest Press Club, which is why this newsletter exists at all. He was known for his optimism, his insistence on using complete sentences, and his belief that the community's moaning practices could be "significantly improved with basic vocal coaching." He leaves behind a partially finished guide to Undead Diction, which this newsletter may eventually publish if someone can decipher the handwriting. He will not be forgotten, mostly because several community members do not retain new information efficiently and will continue to expect him at meetings.
Lorraine Bumble
Born: 1958 · First Departure: 2021 · Second Departure: This Past Tuesday
Lorraine joined the community after a brief period of confusion and adjustment and quickly became known for her considerable energy, her strong opinions about the potluck rotation, and her refusal to accept that the potluck had been permanently canceled. She organized three unofficial potlucks, two of which went well and one of which is the reason the potluck is now officially and permanently canceled. She was enthusiastic, community-minded, and is remembered fondly by those who were not at the third potluck.
Anonymous Member, Unit 12
Born: Unknown · First Departure: Unknown · Second Departure: Sometime Wednesday
The resident of Unit 12 was not known personally by any member of the obituary committee, who note that they have been meaning to introduce themselves since the community's founding and are sorry they never got around to it. The community extends its condolences, or would if anyone could remember where Unit 12 is.
