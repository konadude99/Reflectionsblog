Storage Wars · Abandoned Units · Believe It or Not

What's Behind
Door No. 47

A field guide to the strangest, most inexplicable, and occasionally illegal things ever found in abandoned storage units — with the caveat that this is a genre saturated in tall tales, and we are not here to ruin the magic.

By Staff · The Peculiar Gazette · Special Investigation Into Other People's Abandoned Stuff

Somewhere in America right now, a stranger is standing in front of a locked storage unit they just bought at auction for $75, a flashlight in one hand, a creeping sense of either dread or euphoria in the other. They don't know what's inside. Nobody does. The previous tenant stopped paying rent, the facility sent notices, nobody answered, and now here we are: this person, that padlock, and whatever is waiting in the dark.

This is the essential promise of the storage unit auction — the industrial-park lottery, the corrugated-steel mystery box. And occasionally, just occasionally, the mystery box contains something that would stop a reasonable person cold. A submarine car. Forty-two snakes. A teddy bear full of cash. A person, or at least the remains of one.

What follows is a catalogue of the finest, strangest, most jaw-dropping finds ever reported from abandoned storage units. Credibility varies. Documentation varies more. The entertainment value, however, is consistent throughout.

THE INDUSTRIAL-PARK LOTTERY:
You PAY $75. You GET a submarine.
(Occasionally.)

Most Cinematic Find · 1989The Lotus That Could Breathe Underwater

There is a hierarchy of storage unit finds, and at its apex sits a single object: the white Lotus Esprit submarine car from the 1977 James Bond film The Spy Who Loved Me. A Long Island couple reportedly paid about $100 for an unclaimed unit in 1989 and found it inside, apparently not knowing what they had. They used it as a storage shed for years — which is, if you think about it, a perfect use for a submarine car.

In 2013, the car went to auction and sold for just under $1 million. The buyer was Elon Musk. He had reportedly wanted it since childhood. This is the most documentable, most cinematic, most perfectly structured story in the entire genre: $100 in, $997,000 out, and a submarine in the middle. It happened once. It will never happen again. But it did happen, and that is enough.

🚗💧
The James Bond Lotus Esprit
Long Island, NY · ~1989 · Verified via auction records

A fully functional submarine car — modified for the film to actually submerge — sat in a rented storage unit for years before its owner realized what they had. It was used, in the interim, as extra storage space. The car was sealed. The car was fine. The car sold for $997,000. The $100 purchase price still stands as the greatest return on investment in storage auction history.

Verdict: The one that started it all. The standard by which all other finds are judged and found slightly wanting.

Most Disturbing Category · OngoingThe Human Remains Section (Somebody Has to Write It)

Let us dispense with this one directly. Human remains have been found in storage units. This is documented, recurring, and deeply unsettling. Urns of cremated ashes appear with some regularity — sometimes one, sometimes several, sometimes what appears to be an entire family's collected dead, packed in boxes and left behind when the rent stopped. Whether this reflects grief, neglect, financial crisis, or some unknowable combination of the three is not for us to say.

In 2022, a woman who won a storage unit at online auction reported finding not only cremated remains but a human fetus among the contents. This is the kind of sentence that stops a reader cold, and properly so. There is no whimsy available here. We note it, we acknowledge it, and we move on — because the alternative is sitting with it, and that seems like more than any of us signed up for today.

⚱️
Urns. Many Urns.
Various Locations · Various Years · Recurring

The storage unit as accidental mausoleum. The reasons people leave their loved ones' ashes in rented units — and then stop paying the rent — are presumably varied and private. The units, once opened, offer no explanation. Just the urns, and the silence, and the question you can't quite bring yourself to ask.

Verdict: The strangest category, and the only one with no punchline. Move along.

Live Animals · California (Naturally)Things That Were Alive and Should Not Have Been There

One of the more reliably reported subcategories of strange storage finds involves animals — specifically, animals that had no business living in a 10-by-10 climate-controlled box on the edge of a parking lot, and yet were doing so anyway.

A California storage unit was reportedly found to contain a live alligator, connected to a broader situation involving drugs and firearms that had, by that point, become significantly less interesting than the alligator. The alligator was, by all accounts, fine. It had opinions about being found.

🐊
One Live Alligator (California)
California · Year Disputed · Reported Broadly

The alligator was allegedly connected to a drugs-and-firearms situation, which is either the most on-brand or most off-brand detail imaginable depending on your frame of reference. Authorities reportedly discovered it alongside the other contraband. The alligator reportedly did not understand why this was a problem.

Verdict: "America has outdone itself again" is a legitimate response here.
🐍🐍🐍
42 Ball Pythons (Also California)
California · Reported · Multiple Sources

Forty-two ball pythons, kept in a storage unit, presumably by someone who had run out of room at home or had never had a home and had always intended for a storage unit to serve as a reptile warehouse. Ball pythons are not dangerous. Forty-two of them in a 10-by-10 storage unit is a different kind of problem entirely — one that is logistical, ethical, and deeply hard to explain to the facility manager.

Verdict: If you like snakes, "forgotten reptile warehouse" is still not the storage-auction dream.

The California Problem

It should be noted that a disproportionate number of live-animal storage unit stories originate in California. This may reflect population density, reporting patterns, the state's outsized role in the national storage unit market, or something deeper and more philosophical about the California condition. We have no data. We have a pattern. We present it without comment.

Please Call Authorities · VariousThings That Explode, Or Could

Somewhere between "legally ambiguous" and "evacuate the building," there is a category of storage unit finds that consists of weapons, ordnance, and explosive devices of unclear vintage and stability. Buyers have reported finding grenades. Antique military hardware. Firearms in various states of legality. Stacks of ammunition. Items for which the correct response is, according to every emergency management professional who has ever been interviewed about this: put it down, back away, and call the police.

The incorrect response — the one that is tempting, the one that people occasionally attempt — is to assess its auction value. This is inadvisable. The grenade does not care about your investment horizon.

💣
Live Ordnance, Grenades, Antique Weapons
Various States · Recurrent · Police Involved

The number of storage units that have yielded genuinely dangerous military hardware is nonzero and possibly higher than most people would find comfortable to contemplate. The items range from antique but operable weapons to grenades whose status as "live" or "inert" is a question best answered by a bomb disposal technician and not by the person holding them in a parking lot.

Verdict: Back away slowly. Call someone with a robot arm and a blast suit. This is not negotiable.

Financial Irregularity · VariousThe Bear Full of Money

In the taxonomy of storage unit legends, the teddy bear full of cash holds a special place — it is the find that contains within it the purest version of the dream. You pay $10 for a storage unit. Inside is a teddy bear. Inside the teddy bear: $300,000 in cash. The story is less clinically documented than the Bond car, but it endures because it is the exact story people want to be true.

Cash has also been found in less whimsical containers — bags, suitcases, safes, and in one case a functional ATM machine that had been stolen and stored, presumably while someone figured out what to do next. They apparently never figured it out.

🧸💵
A Teddy Bear Containing $300,000
Exact Location Disputed · Story Widely Circulated · Documentation Thin

A $10 storage unit purchase. A stuffed animal. Approximately $300,000 in cash inside the stuffed animal. The story lacks the evidentiary trail of the Bond car. The story does not care. The story does not need your documentary standards. The story needs only one thing: to be exactly the kind of thing that could happen, which it is. That is enough.

Verdict: Possibly true. Certainly perfect. We're going with it.

Famous People's Stuff · DocumentedThe Burt Reynolds Files

Celebrity memorabilia has a way of dispersing after death — through estate sales, through gifting, through donation, and through the particular entropy that sets in when a famous person's possessions outlast their owner's organizational capacity. Burt Reynolds, who died in 2018, left behind a collection large enough that items have been turning up at auction ever since: screen-worn costumes, signed photographs, personal effects, trophies, props from his films, clothing, and objects that raise the question of what, exactly, counts as memorabilia when you're Burt Reynolds and your whole life was a kind of performance.

The storage unit connection is a common vector for this kind of dispersal. Somebody stored something, somebody forgot it, somebody found it, and now it is on eBay. This is the circle of celebrity-adjacent life.

🎬⭐
Celebrity Memorabilia Hoards
Burt Reynolds · Various Others · Post-Mortem Dispersal

Screen-worn clothing. Signed awards. Props. Personal objects. The famous person's life, itemized and boxed, slowly becoming someone else's auction find. Burt Reynolds memorabilia has appeared through multiple channels over the years. The storage unit is not always the vector, but it is a known one — and there is something melancholy and strange about the idea of a star's legacy sitting in a corrugated metal box, waiting for someone to come and put a price on it.

Verdict: Less bizarre than reptiles. Stranger in the "why was this left behind?" sense.

Retail Therapy Gone Wrong · Portland, OR$410 In, $70,000 Out

A Portland reseller reportedly paid $410 for a storage unit and found inside: hundreds of pairs of high-end shoes, fur coats, and designer clothing estimated to be worth approximately $70,000. This is, by any measure, an excellent outcome. It is also deeply mysterious — designer clothing worth $70,000 does not walk into a storage unit by itself, and someone presumably noticed it was missing. The unit provided no answers. The unit never does.

👠🧥
$70,000 in Designer Clothing
Portland, OR · Reseller Discovery · Reported

Fur coats. Hundreds of pairs of designer shoes. An estimated $70,000 in high-end fashion, stored in a unit that cost $410 to acquire at auction. The backstory is unknown. The provenance is unclear. The shoes fit, or they don't. This is not a question the buyer stopped to ask for long.

Verdict: Second-best return on investment in storage unit history. The Bond car is still number one.

Inexplicable Obsessions · Various10,000 Rubber Ducks and Other Very Specific Commitments

Perhaps the most fundamentally human category of storage unit find is not the valuables, not the contraband, not even the remains — it is the collection. The evidence of a single person's very specific, very sustained fixation with one type of object, pursued across years or decades, and then abandoned to a storage unit that eventually went unclaimed.

Thousands of rubber ducks. Every issue of a single magazine for sixty years. A full-scale working pipe organ. A room-sized model train layout, assembled inside the unit. A knight's complete suit of armor. Dozens of antique prosthetic limbs. Hundreds of clown suits. A life-size replica of the Batmobile. Thousands and thousands of golf balls. The teddy bear collection that stretched the entire length of one wall and wrapped around two others. These are not inventories. These are portraits of people.

🦆🦆🦆
The Rubber Duck Hoard (≈10,000)
Location Various · Collectors Abound

Someone bought 10,000 rubber ducks. Presumably over time. Presumably with intention. And then rented a storage unit, filled it with 10,000 rubber ducks, and at some point stopped paying the rent — leaving behind an army of small yellow birds in permanent, silent formation. The next buyer opened the unit and confronted an unimaginable quantity of cheerful waterfowl staring back at them. We hope they kept at least one.

Verdict: Chaotic. Harmless. A portrait of a person we would have liked to meet.
🎠🚂
A Room-Sized Model Train Layout
Multiple Reports · Assembled In Situ

Not a boxed model train set. Not the components of a model train layout. A complete, assembled, room-sized model train landscape, built inside the unit — hills, tunnels, stations, tiny figures, the whole enterprise — and then left there when the rent stopped. Whether the trains still ran is not recorded. We would like to believe they did.

Verdict: Someone built a world and then left it. This is either the saddest or most romantic find in the list. Possibly both.
🤡🤡
Hundreds of Clown Suits
Various Reports · Context Absent

Hundreds of them. Full clown suits, presumably with accessories. The question of whether this represented a professional clown's career inventory, a themed collector's life's work, or something else entirely is unanswerable. The suits are just there, in the dark, waiting. They are not doing anything. They are simply clown suits. In their quantity, they become something else.

Verdict: Terrifying without any effort. Context would not help here. Context might make it worse.

Final ThoughtsWhat It All Means, and Why It Doesn't

The storage unit is, when you think about it, a peculiar institution. It is rented space for the overflow of life — the things that don't fit in the house, the things we're not ready to throw away, the things we'll deal with later. "Later" does not always come. Rent stops being paid. Lives move on, fall apart, or simply end. And someone else's overflow becomes, eventually, a mystery box in a parking lot auction.

What distinguishes the strangest finds is not their weirdness in isolation — a submarine car is just a car, a rubber duck is just a duck — but the inexplicable gap between the object and the context. Why was the Lotus Esprit in a Long Island storage unit? Why were there 42 ball pythons? Why did someone build a model train world and then walk away from it? The storage unit answers none of these questions. It just holds the evidence that the questions exist.

The most cinematic find is the Bond car. The most horrifying category is human remains. The most quintessentially human is the collection — the evidence of a single obsession, pursued faithfully across years, and then released into the mystery-box economy of a storage auction at $75 a pop.

Someone is standing in front of a locked unit right now. They don't know what's inside. It's probably furniture. It might be nothing at all. But somewhere in the vast corrugated-metal archipelago of American self-storage, a door is about to open onto something no one was expecting. That door is always about to open. This is the promise. This is the whole thing.
