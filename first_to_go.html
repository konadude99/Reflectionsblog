Deeper Reflections

The First
to Go

On being the first in your family to go to college — the cultural dislocation, the imposter syndrome, the changed vocabulary, the weight of everyone’s hope, and, at the end, the pride that belongs not only to you.

Essay Deeper Reflections Series Reading time: 14 min

“You are going. You will make something of yourself. You will do what we could not do. Do not let us down.” — The weight of every first-generation student’s departure, spoken or not.

The leaving that changes everything, in both directions.  |  Shutterstock

There is a particular kind of leaving that has no ceremony adequate to it. Not a wedding, not a funeral, not a graduation — though graduation will come later, if things go well, and will carry its own complicated freight. This is the earlier leaving: the one where you put your things in a bag or a box or the back of someone’s borrowed truck and go somewhere your parents have never been, to study things they may not fully understand, in a language that is yours and theirs and somehow, in ways you cannot yet anticipate, will slowly become more yours than theirs. The leaving that changes you in ways you will not be able to explain at Thanksgiving, and will not know how to undo, and would not undo if you could, and will feel guilty about not wanting to undo.

This is the leaving of the first in the family to go to college. It is not a triumphant story, though it may end in triumph. It is, at its core, a story about being in two places at once — or rather, about belonging completely to neither. About arriving somewhere enormous and discovering, in the first week, that you do not know things that everyone else appears to have been born knowing: which fork, which tone of voice, how to speak to a professor, how to ask for help in a building designed for people who have never needed to ask. And about going home for the holidays to find that the place you left has not moved, but you have, and the distance between you is now measured not in miles but in a vocabulary that lands differently at the dinner table than it does in the seminar room.

I.

The Weight Before the Beginning

The weight arrives before the classes do. It arrives, in fact, before the acceptance letter does, in the understanding — absorbed from the family’s atmosphere over years, never quite stated, never quite unstated — that your going represents something larger than your own ambition. That you carry with you the unlived possibilities of people who worked too hard and too young to have any possibilities of their own. That the money scraped together for the application fee, or the tuition, or the books, is not merely money but compressed sacrifice, converted into hope and placed in your keeping.

For the child of immigrants, the weight has a particular shape. Your parents left a country, a language, a web of people who knew them entirely, and came to a place where they were perpetually beginning, perpetually proving, perpetually foreign in ways that never fully resolved. They endured this in the explicit belief — sometimes spoken, always present — that the children would not have to. That the children would belong somewhere in a way they never quite did. The college acceptance letter is, to them, a down payment on that original bargain. You are not simply going to college. You are the reason they came.

For an Indigenous person going to a college hundreds of miles from a nation with its own language, its own stories, its own complete and ancient way of ordering the world, the weight has a different but equally profound shape. The education being offered is the education of the dominant culture — valuable, potentially, but also the education of the people who spent a century trying to undo exactly the things that make you who you are. You arrive not simply as a student but as a kind of emissary, carrying your community’s investment in you and your own uncertain relationship to what it might mean to succeed in terms that were never written with you in mind.

For the child of a family in poverty, or a family where college was not within the frame of what seemed possible — not a dream deferred so much as a dream that never formed, because the conditions for forming it were never present — the weight is the weight of having stepped outside the story everyone around you is living. You are, in some quiet and unsettling way, already an exception before you have done anything exceptional. The act of going is already a departure from the expected trajectory of your life, and departures from expected trajectories require energy that your classmates, whose trajectories always included this step, do not have to spend.

II.

Arriving in a Country You Were Not Prepared For

The campus is not a neutral space. It is a place built by particular people, for particular people, over a long time, and the accumulated assumptions of that building are embedded everywhere — in the architecture, in the rituals, in the unspoken codes of behavior that everyone seems to know and no one seems to have taught. How to email a professor (and that you should, and what tone to use, and that “Mr.” is wrong and “Dr.” is right and “Professor” is safest). How to speak in a seminar — not too much, not too little, with the right blend of confidence and deference that marks you as serious but not threatening, engaged but not desperate. How to ask for an extension, a recommendation, a clarification, in a register that implies you belong here and merely require information.

These are not small things. They are the grammar of an institution, and like all grammars they are easiest to learn when you grow up speaking them. The student whose parents went to college — whose parents’ parents went to college — has been receiving instruction in this grammar since childhood, at dinner tables where professors were discussed without awe, in households where books were unremarkable furniture, in a general atmosphere that said, quietly and constantly: this is your world too. The first-generation student arrives in the same classroom and has to decode, in real time and under pressure, a system of signals that their peers absorbed without effort and without noticing that they had absorbed it.

What the research finds

Studies consistently find that first-generation college students experience higher rates of anxiety, depression, and what psychologists call “imposter syndrome” — the persistent, irrational conviction that you do not truly belong where you are, that your admission was an error, and that you will shortly be found out. This conviction is not merely a feeling; it is an energy expenditure. Every hour spent managing the fear of exposure is an hour not spent on the actual work of learning.

Research at Stanford and elsewhere has found that one of the most powerful interventions is simply telling first-generation students that what they are feeling is normal, that it passes, and that the very qualities that made them feel out of place — resourcefulness, adaptability, the ability to navigate multiple worlds — are significant intellectual strengths. The knowledge that others felt it too does not eliminate the feeling, but it stops the feeling from compounding into shame.

The imposter syndrome of the first-generation student is not identical to the garden-variety self-doubt that afflicts most new students. It is more specifically targeted. It does not merely say: I may not be smart enough for this. It says: I may not be the kind of person this place was built for. And that is a different and lonelier fear, because it cannot be answered by studying harder or writing a better paper. It can only be answered by the slow and difficult accumulation of evidence that you belong here — evidence that takes time to gather, time during which the doubt continues its quiet work.

III.

Going Home with a Changed Vocabulary

The holiday table is where the cultural dislocation becomes most acute, because it is where both worlds are simultaneously present and cannot be kept separate. You return to your family’s house carrying four months of a new way of thinking, a new set of references, a new vocabulary that has been building in you like a second language — except that it is not a second language but a second register of the one language you all share, and the difference is somehow more disorienting than an actual translation would be.

You use a word that has always meant one thing at home and that you have come to use more precisely in a different context, and someone at the table makes a joke about it, and the joke is gentle and loving and still lands like a small sharp thing. You try to explain something you have learned and find that the explanation requires other explanations, and by the third layer of context you can see something shifting in the faces of the people you love most — not dismissal, not resentment, but something more difficult: the awareness, on both sides simultaneously, that you are no longer entirely speaking the same language.

And so you translate yourself, all through the holiday — editing what you say before you say it, reaching for the version of yourself that fits here, that does not require explanation, that has not yet started using the word “epistemological” in casual conversation. You love these people completely and you also cannot, for the life of you, explain what you did in your anthropology seminar last Tuesday. The two things are simultaneously true and the space between them is where a particular kind of loneliness lives.

What no one tells you — what cannot really be told in advance, only survived — is that this loneliness is not a sign that you have done something wrong. It is the predictable consequence of having done something right. Growth is always, in some degree, a departure. And departures always involve a distance that did not exist before. The distance does not mean you love your family less. It means you have become, partially and painfully, someone they have not entirely met yet — someone who contains them and exceeds them, not in worth but in a particular kind of exposure, and who will spend years learning how to hold both without losing either.

On code-switching and the cost of it

Psychologists and linguists use the term “code-switching” to describe the practice of shifting between languages, dialects, or registers depending on context. For first-generation students — and especially for those whose racial, ethnic, or class identity marks them as outsiders in a predominantly white or affluent campus culture — code-switching is not a choice but a survival skill, and it carries a measurable cognitive and emotional cost.

The energy spent deciding which version of yourself to present, monitoring how you are being perceived, managing the gap between where you are from and where you are — this is energy not available for other things. Students who must code-switch extensively are, in a very literal sense, working harder than their peers without receiving academic credit for it. The campus that does not see this tax is a campus that mistakes equal access for equal conditions.

IV.

What Is Lost and What Is Not Lost

There is a version of the first-generation college story that is told as pure ascent — the child who escaped, who rose, who made it out and made it good. This version is not false, but it is incomplete, because it frames the family and the community of origin as a condition to be overcome rather than a place that gave you something and continues to hold something of you. The ascent narrative is the story that the dominant culture finds easiest to celebrate because it requires nothing of the dominant culture: it simply absorbs you, and congratulates itself on your absorption.

What is actually lost is real and worth grieving. The easiness of full belonging somewhere. The relief of not having to explain yourself. The particular kind of rest that comes from being among people who know your references, your humor, your family’s particular way of moving through the world, without any of it having to be translated or qualified or gently contextualized for an audience unfamiliar with it. These are not small losses. They are the texture of being at home, and once the texture has been disturbed it does not restore itself completely, not in either direction.

But what is not lost is also real. The family does not lose you because you have changed; they gain a version of you that is larger than the one that left, even if the larger version is sometimes harder to hold. The community that sent you does not become irrelevant because you have gone elsewhere to learn; it becomes, if you carry it with you honestly, the ground on which your thinking stands — the place where the questions you are being trained to ask actually matter to actual people in actual lives. The things you learned at home — how to work without a net, how to make something from not enough, how to move between worlds, how to read a room with the precision of someone whose comfort was never guaranteed — these are not deficits that college must overcome. They are capacities that college, if it is paying attention, ought to envy.

V.

The Pride That Belongs to Everyone

And then, if things go as they sometimes do, there is the graduation.

It is worth saying clearly what the graduation of a first-generation student actually is, beneath the gowns and the processional and the speeches about futures and responsibilities. It is the moment at which a family’s long investment in the possible becomes visible. Everything that was given up so that you could go — the overtime, the second job, the dream quietly set aside, the country left behind, the language half-lost in the crossing — resolves, for one morning in a folding chair on a lawn or in a gymnasium, into the sight of you in a cap and gown, receiving a piece of paper that your parents could not receive and that they wanted, more than you may ever fully understand, for you.

The pride of that moment is not yours alone. This is what distinguishes first-generation graduation from most other forms of achievement: the pride radiates outward with unusual force. Your parents are proud of you, yes — profoundly, tearfully, in ways they may not have words for — but they are also proud of themselves, as they should be, for having made it possible. Your grandparents, living or dead, are somewhere in the room. Your community is in the room. Everyone who ever said you could when the evidence was thin is in the room, and the room is very full.

A note on what comes after

First-generation graduates often describe a disorientation after graduation that they did not anticipate: the sense of not knowing what the next chapter is supposed to look like, because there is no family script for it. Their parents could tell them what high school would be like because they had been to high school. They cannot tell them what to do after a bachelor’s degree, because that map was never drawn in their household.

This is not a failure. It is the beginning of something the family builds together, in real time, without a template. The first-generation graduate who becomes a professional, a scholar, a person of standing in whatever field chose them — that person is writing a new chapter in a family story. The chapter their children and grandchildren will read as the beginning of something that was always meant to happen.

The person who completes a college education as the first in their family to do so has not simply earned a degree. They have done something harder than that: they have lived, for four years or more, in the uncomfortable space between two worlds without fully belonging to either, and they have endured the particular loneliness of being the one who goes, and they have kept going anyway. They have translated themselves back and forth across a cultural distance that no one measured and no campus orientation addressed. They have carried the weight of everyone’s hope without being crushed by it — or, in some cases, after being crushed by it, gotten back up.

And they have brought something back. Not the degree, though the degree matters. Something less tangible: the knowledge, now embodied in a person who is real and present and sitting at the holiday table, that it can be done. That someone from this family, from this community, from this set of circumstances that was never supposed to produce this outcome — can do it. The first-generation graduate is the proof of concept. Every younger sibling, every neighbor’s child, every kid down the street who is trying to figure out if someone like them is allowed to want this — they now have an answer. The answer is you.

You went somewhere your family had never been. You learned things in a language that was yours and theirs and slowly became more yours. You came home changed, and changed again at home, and held both changes as best you could. You were lonely in ways you could not explain and proud in ways you could not quite say. You did not let anyone down. You carried everyone with you. And when they put the hood on your shoulders and called your name, the person who stood to receive it was not only you — it was every person in your family who ever worked too hard, hoped too quietly, and gave something up so that this moment might exist. They were there. They are always there. You carry them the way you carry your first language: beneath everything, fluent, permanent, yours.
